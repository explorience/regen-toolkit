# Funding Your Gatherings

*Practical strategies for funding community events - from zero budget to grant-funded*

---

## The Most Important Principle

Most community gatherings don't need money. They need people, a space, and a purpose. If you're waiting until you have funding to start gathering, you'll never start.

The best community events in the world are shared meals, park meetups, and living room conversations that cost nothing. Money helps with scale and comfort, but it's never the prerequisite. Your community's most valuable resources are time, skills, and goodwill - none of which require a budget line.

That said, as your local node grows, you'll want to host events that require some investment: workshops with materials, larger gatherings with food, events in proper venues, or multi-day events with speakers. Here's how to fund them at every level.

---

## Tier 1: Zero Budget

These formats cost nothing and can be remarkably effective:

### Shared Meals

Everyone brings something to contribute. This format distributes both cost and effort, and the act of bringing food creates personal investment in the gathering. People who contribute something feel more ownership than people who show up to a catered event.

In many East African communities, communal meals where everyone brings a dish are already a regular practice. You don't need to invent this - you need to make it consistent and connect it to your node's purpose.

**Tips for good shared meals:**
- Use a shared sign-up system (a group chat thread or shared list) so you don't end up with all one type of food
- Always have one backup person committed to bringing a main dish in case sign-ups are sparse
- Dietary accommodations: ask people to label their dishes
- Keep it simple. This isn't a dinner party - it's community building.

### Free Venue Strategies

- **Public libraries**: Most have free meeting rooms you can reserve. Some have projectors and whiteboards.
- **Parks and public spaces**: Weather permitting, outdoor gatherings are free and naturally casual. Bring a mat, find a shaded area.
- **Member homes**: Living room or courtyard meetups create intimacy. Rotate hosts to share the load.
- **Partner organizations**: Community centres, mosques, churches, and nonprofits often have meeting space they'll share for free if your mission aligns with theirs.
- **Tea houses and cafés**: Many have back rooms or off-peak hours where they'll let groups meet if members buy drinks. This costs each person a small amount but costs the organizer nothing.
- **University spaces**: If any members are students or faculty, campus rooms can often be reserved for community use.

### Skill Contributions

Instead of hiring someone to facilitate, teach, or provide food - find community members who can contribute their skills. A member who's a professional facilitator can run your governance workshop. A member who bakes can bring bread. A member with a speaker system can provide sound.

This approach has a double benefit: it costs nothing AND it deepens members' investment in the community. When people contribute their skills, they become co-creators rather than consumers of the event.

## Tier 2: Low Budget ($10-100)

When you need a little money but not much:

### Member Contributions

**Sliding scale**: Suggest a small contribution per person, but make it genuinely optional with a "pay what you can" model. Never turn anyone away for lack of funds. The language matters: "Suggested contribution: $10. All are welcome regardless of ability to pay." In contexts where even small fees create barriers, be flexible and consider whether the sliding scale truly works or just creates awkwardness.

**Pass the hat**: At the event itself, pass around a container or set up a mobile payment option. Low pressure, people give what feels right. This typically raises enough for basic event costs at a 15-20 person gathering.

**Monthly dues**: If your node has a regular gathering schedule, small monthly dues create a predictable event fund. With 20 members contributing a modest amount each month, you can cover most community gathering costs consistently.

### What a Small Budget Buys
- Food and drinks for 15-20 people
- Printed materials for a workshop
- A basic venue rental for 2-3 hours
- Supplies for a hands-on activity (seed kits for a garden workshop, craft supplies for a community art project)
- A small honorarium for a local speaker or facilitator

### Crypto-Native Collection

Use a simple multisig wallet or a platform like Juicebox to collect small contributions from members. This serves a dual purpose: it funds the event AND gives members practice using Web3 tools. Even collecting a small amount in USDC from each member is a learning moment.

For communities new to crypto, the act of collecting event contributions on-chain can be the most practical introduction to digital wallets and transactions. It makes the abstract concrete: "This is how we're going to manage our community funds."

## Tier 3: Medium Budget ($100-500)

For larger events, workshops with materials, or events requiring proper venues:

### Local Business Sponsorship

Local businesses often sponsor community events in exchange for visibility. This works best when there's genuine alignment - a local café sponsoring a community dinner, a co-working space hosting a workshop, a bookshop providing a reading space.

**What to offer sponsors:**
- Their name or logo on event materials and social posts
- Public thank-you during the event
- Table or display at the event
- Introduction to your community members (potential customers)
- Photos of their space or contribution in use (useful for their own marketing)

**How to approach:**
- Start with businesses you already use - warm connections work better than cold asks
- Be specific about what you need: "$200 for food for 40 people" is clearer than "sponsorship"
- Offer something concrete in return, not vague "exposure"
- Follow through: actually thank them publicly, share photos, write a genuine note of appreciation

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't approach businesses you've never visited
- Don't promise things you can't deliver (attendance numbers you're guessing at)
- Don't make it purely transactional - frame it as mutual community investment

### Small Grants

Several types of organizations offer small grants for community events:

- **Local community foundations**: Most cities and regions have foundations that fund community activities. Applications are usually straightforward.
- **Municipal arts and culture grants**: Local governments often have small grants for community gatherings, especially if there's an educational or cultural component.
- **Cooperative development funds**: If your node has a cooperative structure, development funds often support member education and community outreach.
- **Diaspora community organizations**: For communities with diaspora connections, these organizations sometimes fund local community-building activities.

### Web3-Native Funding

- **Gitcoin Grants**: If your local node has a Gitcoin profile, you can receive quadratic funding. Quadratic funding amplifies small contributions - even $1 donations count because the matching formula rewards breadth of support, not just total amount raised.
- **Giveth**: List your event on Giveth to receive crypto donations from the broader regen community. Giveth also supports recurring donations through Superfluid streaming.
- **Endaoment**: For communities with charitable status or working through a fiscal sponsor, Endaoment provides a way for crypto donors to make tax-deductible contributions through donor-advised funds. This bridges the gap between crypto wealth and community needs.
- **DAO treasury**: If your node has a treasury, allocating a portion to community events is one of the highest-return investments you can make. Events build the social infrastructure that everything else depends on. A DAO that doesn't invest in gatherings is like a company that doesn't invest in its office - the work technically continues, but the culture erodes.

## Tier 4: Large Budget ($500+)

For multi-day events, conferences, or significant community gatherings:

### Event-Scale Funding

For events with 50 or more attendees, you'll likely need $1,000-5,000 or more. This requires more structured fundraising:

- **Ticket sales**: Even small fees add up. Always offer free or reduced tickets for those who can't pay. Some events use a "pay it forward" model where those who can afford more cover tickets for those who can't.
- **Multiple sponsors**: Organize your sponsorship in tiers (for example, Gold, Silver, Bronze) with different levels of visibility and involvement at each level.
- **Speaker budgets**: If you're bringing in speakers from outside your community, budget for travel and honoraria. $200-500 per speaker is standard for community events. For local speakers, even a small honorarium shows respect for their time and expertise.
- **Grants**: Apply 3-6 months in advance. Ethereum Foundation, Protocol Labs, and various Web3 ecosystem grants fund community education events. Artizen, Octant, and Karma Seeds are also worth exploring for event funding.

### Budget Template for a 50-Person Event

Here's a rough budget at three spending levels:

**Low budget (about $300 total):**
- Venue: $0 (donated space)
- Food and drinks: $200 (community-cooked or simple catering)
- Materials and printing: $50
- Speakers: $0 (community members)
- AV and tech: $0 (borrowed equipment)
- Contingency: $50

**Medium budget (about $1,100 total):**
- Venue: $200
- Food and drinks: $400
- Materials and printing: $100
- Speaker honoraria: $200
- AV and tech: $100
- Contingency: $100

**High budget (about $2,500 total):**
- Venue: $500
- Food and drinks: $800
- Materials and printing: $200
- Speaker honoraria: $500
- AV and tech: $300
- Contingency: $200

## Financial Transparency

Whatever your budget level, be transparent about money. Community trust erodes fast when finances are not visible.

**Best practices:**
- Share your budget publicly before the event (what you plan to spend)
- Share actual expenses after the event (what you actually spent)
- If you have surplus funds, decide together what to do with them
- Use transparent tools: a shared spreadsheet, an on-chain treasury, or at minimum a public accounting that anyone can review
- Never let one person handle money without accountability to the group

This is where Web3 tools can genuinely help. An on-chain treasury with multisig approval means no one person can move funds without others signing off. That's not just good technology - it's good governance. Even for a small event budget, the habit of financial transparency builds the trust infrastructure for larger treasury management later.

## The Sustainability Question

The real challenge isn't funding one event - it's creating a sustainable funding model for ongoing gatherings.

**Models that work long-term:**
- **Membership dues** fund regular events predictably
- **Rotating sponsors** spread the ask across multiple businesses over time
- **Treasury allocation** dedicates a percentage of node funds to community events
- **Hybrid model**: Free regular events (shared meals, park meetups) plus funded quarterly events (workshops, celebrations)

The key insight: don't overspend on events early. Communities that start with expensive, polished events struggle when the budget runs out. Communities that start with free shared meals and gradually introduce funded events as they grow build sustainable rhythms.

This mirrors how healthy organizations grow in general: start lean, prove the model, then invest. A community that has been gathering for free for six months and then asks for $500 for a workshop has credibility. A community that leads with a $5,000 launch event and then can't sustain monthly meetups has a problem.

## Try This

**Create a funding ladder for your next 6 months of gatherings.**

Map out your planned gatherings and assign a funding tier to each:

1. **Month 1-2**: Two zero-budget gatherings (shared meals, park meetups). Purpose: build the habit of gathering.
2. **Month 3**: One low-budget gathering ($50-100). Collect member contributions. Purpose: test your community's willingness to invest.
3. **Month 4-5**: One medium-budget gathering ($200-500). Approach one local business for sponsorship. Purpose: build external relationships.
4. **Month 6**: One larger gathering. Apply for a small grant or allocate from your treasury. Purpose: celebrate what you've built and attract new members.

For each gathering, answer these three questions before you start spending:
- What is the minimum budget that achieves our purpose?
- Where can we substitute skill contributions for cash?
- How will we account for and report on the money spent?

Track what works and what doesn't. After 6 months, you'll have real data on what your community's gathering economics look like, rather than guesses.
