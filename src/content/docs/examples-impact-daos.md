# Real Examples of Impact DAOs

*How communities are using web3 tools to regenerate the world*

---

## What Are Impact DAOs?

If you have ever been part of a permaculture design course, a seed swap, or a neighbourhood tool library, you have already experienced something like a DAO in action. A DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization) is simply a group of people who share a mission and make decisions together — with some help from digital tools that make coordination easier.

Impact DAOs take this further. They are groups using these tools specifically to create positive change — whether that is restoring ecosystems, funding local projects, or building new economic systems that work for everyone.

The key difference from traditional organisations? No central boss. Everyone who joins can have a voice in decisions, and the rules are written into code that everyone can see. Think of it like a cooperative that runs itself, where trust is built into the system rather than relying on one person to manage everything.

## Real Examples You Can Understand

### ReFi DAO: Local Roots, Global Reach

ReFi DAO is one of the most accessible examples for permaculture and regenerative community folks. They are building a "regenerative economy" that starts with local communities.

Imagine if your local group could connect with similar groups around the world — not just to chat, but to share resources, fund each other's projects, and learn from each other's successes and failures. That is what ReFi DAO does.

They have helped start Local Nodes in places like Costa Rica, Barcelona, Tanzania, and Mexico. Each node focuses on local environmental challenges:

- **ReFi Costa Rica**: Merges crypto philanthropy with grassroots activism
- **ReFi Tanzania**: Addresses urban waste through cleanup systems
- **ReFi BayArea**: Integrates solar energy with regenerative real estate

What makes this powerful is that these local nodes are not just isolated projects. They are part of a larger network where ideas, funding, and support flow between them. A technique that works in Costa Rica can inspire a project in Tanzania. Money raised in Barcelona can support work in Lagos.

This is exactly how permaculture has spread — through networks of practitioners sharing knowledge locally while staying connected globally.

### Greenpill: Turning Interest into Action

Greenpill started with a simple idea: what if people who were already engaged with crypto could redirect that energy toward regenerative projects?

They call it "Turning Degens to Regens" — moving from speculation toward building something that actually helps communities flourish.

Greenpill works through local chapters worldwide. Each chapter organises grassroots activities in their region, bringing regenerative web3 to local communities. They have published books exploring everything from local currency systems to how communities in Kenya manage capital — which connects directly to permaculture's emphasis on local economic resilience.

The movement has several participation levels:

- **Beginner**: Learn and connect socially
- **Intermediate**: Share with friends
- **Advanced**: Start an ImpactDAO, organise teachbacks, run regenerative experiments

This mirrors how many permaculture teachers got started — one person learns something valuable, then teaches a friend, then organises a workshop, then eventually starts a full design practice.

### Klima DAO: Carbon Markets for the People

Klima DAO tackles climate change by making carbon offsets more accessible. They use a bridge that takes verified carbon credits and brings them onto the blockchain where anyone can trade them.

The vision is to create a transparent, liquid market for carbon credits that anyone can participate in — not just big corporations. Imagine a world where your local land restoration project could earn carbon credits that are automatically tracked, verified, and sellable to anyone, anywhere.

If you are doing regenerative agriculture or forest restoration, you are sequestering carbon. Currently, capturing that value is complicated and often requires middlemen. Impact DAOs are exploring ways to let communities capture that value directly.

## Why This Matters for Permaculture

Permaculture has always been about:

1. **Working with natural systems** rather than against them
2. **Creating local resilience** while connecting to broader networks
3. **Sharing resources and knowledge** rather than hoarding
4. **Building economies** that regenerate rather than extract

Impact DAOs are exploring how to use technology to do exactly these things — but at scale, and with new tools.

A DAO can:
- Let a group of land stewards pool money to buy a property together
- Automatically distribute funds to projects based on verified outcomes
- Create transparent systems for sharing resources across continents
- Enable communities to own the infrastructure they depend on

None of this replaces the hands-on work of planting food forests or building swales. But it might make it easier to fund that work, coordinate those efforts, and connect with allies around the world.

## Try This

**Explore One DAO**

1. Visit the ReFi DAO network — look for a Local Node near you or near East Africa. Notice how each node adapts the same core principles to their local context.

2. Think about a project in your own community that could benefit from: easier sharing of resources with groups elsewhere, more transparent decision-making, or new ways to fund regeneration work. You do not need to build anything tomorrow. Just notice where the friction is in your current collaboration.

3. Browse the Greenpill network resources — they have free guides on topics like local currency systems and local economic resilience. Even if web3 is new to you, the underlying ideas about community finance are worth knowing.

4. If you are part of a savings circle, women's group, or cooperative: think about what tools you already use to coordinate. What works well? What is frustrating? Sometimes the " DAO " is just a better version of tools you already trust.

## The Bigger Picture

Impact DAOs are not a magic solution. They are experiments — some will succeed, others will fail, and everyone is learning. But they are worth watching, and perhaps participating in, because they are asking the same questions permaculture has always asked:

*How do we create systems that regenerate rather than extract? How do we coordinate at scale without losing local wisdom? How do we build economies where everyone benefits?*

The tools are different. The values feel familiar.
