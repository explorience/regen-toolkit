# Funding Your Local Node

*How to finance your community finance system without losing your community*

---

## Why Funding Matters

Money is uncomfortable to talk about. But local nodes need resources — even small ones.

The question is not whether to fund your node. It is how to fund it in a way that matches your values.

Community-owned and community-governed finance should be supported by funding models that reflect those principles. Your funding approach should strengthen your community, not slowly change it into something else.

---

## The Funding Spectrum

Local nodes can be funded in many ways. The right approach depends on your scale, goals, and community preferences.

Here are the options, from simplest to most complex.

---

## Free: The Grassroots Approach

### Member Contributions
The simplest: each member contributes time or money.

Examples:
- A small amount per month from each member
- Skills contributed instead of money
- Equipment or space donations

Pros:
- Simple to start
- Builds ownership
- Keeps everyone equal

Cons:
- Limits who can participate
- Can feel like another subscription

How to set amounts:
- Ask what feels comfortable
- Start low, increase later
- Have a "give what you can" option

### Sweat Equity
Members contribute labour instead of money.

Examples:
- Everyone helps with events
- Skills shared freely
- Manual record-keeping

This works well for communities with more time than money.

Pros:
- Everyone can participate regardless of income
- Builds stronger connections

Cons:
- Can burn people out
- Hard to measure value

---

## Low-Cost: Minimal Structures

### Use Free Tools
You do not need expensive software to run a local node:

- **Communication**: WhatsApp, Signal — widely available in Somaliland and East Africa
- **Tracking**: Notebook, shared document, simple spreadsheet
- **Meetings**: Community spaces, mosques, women's group meeting rooms
- **Storage**: Members' homes

Cost: near zero

### Barter and Exchange
Trade without money entirely:

- Skills for skills
- Items for items
- Time for time

This is how timebanks work. It is also how most communities function informally. Rotating savings groups — called "ayuto" or similar names across East Africa — are a well-established form of this.

### Community Kitchen
If your node involves food:
- Potluck model
- Shared shopping — each person brings something
- Sliding scale contributions

---

## Medium: Building Capacity

### Membership Fees
Formalise contributions with monthly or annual fees.

This is how cooperatives typically work. Members pay to participate.

How to set fees:
1. Calculate your actual costs
2. Divide by expected members
3. Add a buffer for growth
4. Discuss with community
5. Start low, adjust later

Example calculation:
- Meeting space: a small amount per month
- Tools and supplies: a small amount per month
- Buffer
- 10 members = a small amount each per month

### Tiered Membership
Different levels for different contributions:

| Tier | Fee | Includes |
|------|-----|----------|
| Basic | low | Voting, basic access |
| Supporter | medium | Basic plus events |
| Patron | higher | All plus priority access |

This lets people give more if they want without forcing anyone.

### Service Fees
If your node provides services:
- Small transaction fees
- Event admission
- Workshop fees

Keep fees low. The goal is not profit — it is sustainability.

### Local Revenue Generation
Make money locally:

- Sell goods — artisan products, food
- Run events — film nights, workshops
- Rent equipment
- Offer services — accounting, design

This keeps money in the community. The advantage of local revenue is that it comes from the community and stays in the community. The disadvantage is it requires ongoing effort and attention.

### Community-Owned Infrastructure
As your node grows, consider whether the community should own the infrastructure it relies on:

- A shared space — a room, a building, a piece of land
- Shared equipment — tools, vehicles, kitchen facilities
- Shared systems — a community phone, a cooperative shop

Community-owned infrastructure builds long-term resilience. It reduces dependence on external providers and creates a physical centre for your node. It also requires more governance — who maintains it? Who decides how it is used? These are good problems to have.

## Large: External Funding

When you have proven your model, external funding can help you grow.

### Grants
Many programmes fund community finance projects:

**Climate and Regeneration:**
- ReFi DAO Local Node grants
- Gitcoin grants
- Climate Collective funding

**Community Development:**
- Local community foundations
- Credit union community development funds
- Municipal grants

**Web3 and Ecosystem:**
- Protocol grants
- Foundation funding
- Community crowdfunding

How to get grants:

1. **Start small**
   - Small grants are easier to get
   - Build a track record
   - Learn the process

2. **Document everything**
   - What you do
   - Who benefits
   - What you have learned

3. **Build relationships**
   - Talk to funders before applying
   - Go to events
   - Show you are serious

4. **Report honestly**
   - Even when things go wrong
   - Funders respect honesty
   - Builds long-term trust

### Impact Investing
If your node will generate returns:
- Community investment rounds
- Regenerative investment funds
- DAO-style funding

This is for more established nodes. It brings complexity.

---

## What to Avoid

### Do Not Take Big Money Too Early

Funding with strings attached can:
- Pressure fast growth
- Require giving up control
- Shift your mission
- Attract people who want returns, not community

Take external money only when you are stable and ready.

### Do Not Over-Fund

More money means more complexity:
- More reporting
- More expectations
- More stress

Start as simple as possible. Add money when you need it.

### Do Not Forget Sustainability

Ask:
- What happens if grants end?
- Can we generate local revenue?
- What is our backup plan?

### Do Not Let Money Rule

Money is a tool, not the goal. Do not let funding decisions override community decisions.

---

## The Community Finance Principle

Your funding model shapes your community.

| Funding Model | Community Effect |
|---------------|------------------|
| Member fees | High ownership, limits access |
| Grants | Growth but funder priorities |
| Revenue | Sustainability but business focus |
| Sweat equity | High participation, burnout risk |

Choose based on what kind of community you want.

Not all funding is equal. The source of your money affects what you can do with it.

---

## Practical Steps

### Month 1-3: Start Free
- Prove the concept
- Use free tools
- Learn what you actually need

### Month 4-6: Add Member Contributions
- Small fees from committed members
- See what works

### Month 7-12: Formalise
- Set up proper structure if needed
- Build recurring revenue

### Year 2+: Seek External Funding
- Apply for grants
- Consider larger investments
- Scale what works

Do not jump to the end before you have proven the beginning.

## Try This

**Map Your Funding Options**

Answer these five questions honestly:

1. What do we actually need money for right now?
2. What can we do for free?
3. How much would a small monthly contribution from members actually be?
4. Who in our community can give more to support others?
5. What happens if we get no external funding?

Write down the answers. They will tell you where to start.

---

## Funding Models and Governance

Different funding sources affect who controls your node:

- **Member-funded**: Members control decisions
- **Grant-funded**: Funder has influence over direction
- **Revenue-funded**: Customers shape priorities
- **Investor-funded**: Investors have a say

Think carefully about who you want making decisions. Money comes with expectations. External funding often means external priorities entering your community space.

## What If No Money Comes?

If external funding does not arrive — and it often does not — you still have options. Many local nodes have run for years on member contributions alone. The constraint forces creativity and genuine commitment.

The absence of large funding is not a failure. It is useful information, and a clear signal to stay small, stay honest, and stay together until conditions are right.

## Moving Forward

Start where you are. Use what you have. Do what you can.

You do not need money to start. You need people who genuinely care and show up.

The funding will follow if the community is real.
