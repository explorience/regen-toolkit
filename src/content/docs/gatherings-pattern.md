# The Gatherings Pattern

*A framework for designing gatherings that build community, deepen relationships, and create lasting impact*

---

## Beyond "Let's Have a Meetup"

Most community gatherings follow a familiar script: find a venue, set a time, post an invite, hope people show up, give a presentation, do questions, go home. This format works for information transfer. It fails at everything else.

The gatherings pattern is a different approach - one that treats every gathering as an intentional design challenge. As Priya Parker argues in *The Art of Gathering*, "gatherings crackle and flourish when real thought goes into them, when (often invisible) structure is baked into them, and when a host has the curiosity, willingness, and generosity of spirit to try."

For local nodes, gatherings aren't just events - they're the primary way you build the trust, relationships, and shared understanding that community finance systems require. A well-designed gathering can accomplish in two hours what months of online messaging cannot.

---

## The Pattern: Purpose, People, Place, Process, Practice

### 1. Purpose: Why Are We Gathering?

Every gathering needs a specific, clear purpose. Not "to learn about Web3" - that's a topic, not a purpose. A purpose answers: what will be different after this gathering? What change are we trying to create?

**Strong purposes:**
- "To help 10 community members set up their first wallet and make their first transaction"
- "To collectively decide how to allocate our community treasury this quarter"
- "To surface and address tensions in our governance process"
- "To introduce 5 new people to 5 existing members through meaningful conversation"

**Weak purposes:**
- "To talk about blockchain"
- "To hang out"
- "To network"
- "Monthly meetup"

The purpose shapes every subsequent decision. If your purpose is trust-building, you need small groups and vulnerable conversation - not a lecture hall. If your purpose is skill-building, you need hands-on practice - not slides.

### 2. People: Who Needs to Be There?

The guest list is a design choice, not a default. Who you invite determines the dynamics, the conversations, and the outcomes.

**Key questions:**
- Who absolutely must be there for the purpose to be achieved?
- Who would add unexpected value or perspective?
- Who might dominate or derail? (You can still invite them - but design around it.)
- What's the right group size for this purpose?

**Group size guidelines:**
- **4-6 people**: Deep conversation, vulnerability, decision-making
- **8-15 people**: Workshop, skill-building, collaborative design
- **20-40 people**: Community meeting, diverse perspectives, energy
- **50+**: Celebration, inspiration, movement-building (but intimacy is lost)

Don't default to "everyone's invited." Sometimes the most powerful gatherings are small, curated, and intentional. In many East African communities, the most important conversations happen in small groups of elders or trusted community members before being brought to the wider group. That pattern of small-group depth feeding into larger-group decisions is sound design, not exclusion.

### 3. Place: Where Does This Happen?

The space shapes the experience more than most organizers realize. A circle of chairs creates different energy than rows facing a stage. A living room creates different intimacy than a conference room. Sitting under a tree creates a different openness than sitting in a fluorescent-lit hall.

**Space principles:**
- **Match space to purpose**: Vulnerable conversations need comfortable, private spaces. Energetic workshops need room to move. Decision-making needs everyone able to see each other.
- **Arrive early, arrange the space**: Don't accept the default setup. Move furniture. Create the configuration that serves your purpose.
- **Remove barriers**: If people need to be equals, don't have some sitting and some standing. If people need to connect, don't put tables between them.
- **Consider accessibility**: Can everyone physically get there? Is it near public transport or walkable? Can people with limited mobility participate? Is there shade if you're outdoors?

**Outdoor gatherings** work well for trust-building and creative thinking. Natural settings reduce stress, encourage openness, and break the association with formal meetings. Community gardens, parks, courtyards, and shaded gathering spots can be powerful spaces - and they're usually free.

### 4. Process: What Happens and When?

This is where most gatherings succeed or fail. Good process creates the conditions for the purpose to be achieved. Bad process - or no process - produces meandering conversations that frustrate everyone.

**The Arc of a Gathering:**

**Opening (10-15 minutes)**
- Welcome and state the purpose clearly: "We're here because..."
- Acknowledge the space and context
- Set agreements (not "ground rules" - agreements are mutual, rules are imposed)
- Create connection early: a round of names and one personal question works well

**Body (main activity, 60-90 minutes)**
- Structured activities that serve the purpose
- Alternate between small group and full group work
- Build in movement - sitting for 2 hours kills energy in any culture
- Create moments of surprise or delight

**Closing (10-15 minutes)**
- Harvest: What are we taking away?
- Commitments: What are we each doing next?
- Appreciation: What worked? Who contributed?
- Preview: What's coming next?

**Process formats that work well for local nodes:**

- **World Café**: Rotating small-group conversations around key questions. Each round, one person stays as "host" while others rotate to new tables. Good for surfacing diverse perspectives on complex issues.
- **Fishbowl**: An inner circle discusses while an outer circle listens. Anyone from the outer circle can tap in and replace someone in the inner circle. Creates focused dialogue with audience engagement.
- **Open Space Technology**: Participants set the agenda themselves. Whoever shows up is the right people. Whatever happens is the only thing that could have. Effective for large groups with diverse interests.
- **Liberating Structures**: A collection of 33 facilitation methods designed to include everyone and release collective intelligence. Each one takes 10-30 minutes and replaces traditional presentations or managed discussions.
- **Circle Practice**: Sitting in a circle, using a talking piece (an object passed from speaker to speaker). Everyone speaks, everyone listens. Powerful for difficult conversations and decision-making. This format has deep roots in many African and Indigenous traditions.

### 5. Practice: What Happens After?

A gathering isn't complete when people leave - it's complete when the purpose has been fulfilled, and that usually requires follow-through.

**Immediate (within 24 hours):**
- Send a personal thank-you to each participant (a quick message goes a long way)
- Share photos, notes, or outputs from the gathering
- Follow up on any commitments made during the gathering

**Short-term (within a week):**
- Connect people who should talk further
- Share resources mentioned during the gathering
- Document decisions and action items in a place everyone can access
- Collect feedback (a brief survey or personal check-in)

**Long-term (ongoing):**
- Track commitments and follow through on them
- Reference the gathering in future communications ("At our last gathering, we decided...")
- Build on what emerged - don't let insights die in someone's notebook
- Use what you learned to design the next gathering better

## Common Patterns for Local Nodes

### The Onboarding Gathering

**Purpose**: Help 5-10 new people understand what your local node does and make their first contribution.

**Format**: 2 hours. Start with food and informal conversation (30 min). Short overview of the node - not a lecture, a conversation (15 min). Hands-on activity: set up a wallet, make a first transaction, contribute to a decision (45 min). Small group discussion: what excites you? what worries you? (20 min). Close with next steps and how to stay connected (10 min).

### The Governance Gathering

**Purpose**: Make a specific decision together - how to allocate funds, change a policy, onboard new partners.

**Format**: 90 minutes. Check-in round (10 min). Present the decision clearly - what exactly are we deciding? (10 min). Small groups explore options (20 min). Full group discussion using consent-based process (30 min). Decision and commitment (10 min). Close (10 min).

### The Trust-Building Gathering

**Purpose**: Deepen relationships between existing members.

**Format**: Evening gathering, 2.5 hours. Shared meal (45 min). Structured conversation in pairs using a prompt like "What's something you're working on that matters to you but that you rarely talk about?" (30 min). Full circle: what did you hear that resonated? (20 min). Collaborative activity: cook together, make art, play a game (30 min). Close with appreciations (15 min).

### The Learning Gathering

**Purpose**: Build specific skills - wallet setup, proposal writing, facilitation, conflict resolution.

**Format**: 2 hours. Brief context (10 min). Demonstration (15 min). Hands-on practice in pairs or small groups (45 min). Troubleshooting and questions (20 min). Reflection: what clicked? what's still confusing? (15 min). Resources and next steps (15 min).

## Principles That Make It Work

**1. Generous authority.** Someone needs to host - to make decisions about format, timing, and flow. This isn't about control. It's about creating the conditions where others can fully participate without worrying about logistics. A good host serves the group by holding the structure.

**2. Start with connection before content.** People need to feel seen before they can think together. Even 5 minutes of personal check-in transforms the quality of everything that follows. Don't skip this because you're short on time. The check-in IS part of the work.

**3. Design for the quiet ones.** The loudest voices don't have the best ideas. Use pair conversations, written reflections, and small groups to create space for introverts and those who need time to process. In many cultures, the most thoughtful people speak last or speak least. Make room for them.

**4. Honour endings.** How you close matters as much as how you open. A rushed ending leaves people feeling incomplete. A thoughtful closing crystallizes meaning and creates momentum for what comes next.

**5. Iterate.** Every gathering is an experiment. Debrief afterward: what worked? What didn't? What would we change? The best gatherers are perpetual learners, and the retrospective format works as well for gatherings as it does for projects.

## Try This

**Design your next gathering using the full pattern.**

Take a gathering you're already planning (or one you need to plan soon) and work through each element:

1. **Purpose**: Write one sentence that describes what will be different after this gathering.
2. **People**: List who needs to be there by name. What's the right group size? Is anyone missing? Is anyone included by default who doesn't need to be?
3. **Place**: Where will you hold it? Does the space match the purpose? What will you change about the space arrangement?
4. **Process**: Write a rough timeline with opening, body, and closing. What's your main activity? How will you create connection early?
5. **Practice**: What will you do within 24 hours of the gathering ending? Who will follow up on commitments?

Share your design with one other person before the gathering and ask: "Does this serve the purpose?" Their feedback will make it better.

After the gathering, spend 10 minutes with your co-organizers answering: What worked? What didn't? What will we do differently next time? Write down the answers. This is how you get better.
