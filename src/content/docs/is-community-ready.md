# Is Your Community Ready for a Local Node?

*How to honestly assess if your group has what it takes*

---

## The Honest Question

Before starting a local node, ask: is your community actually ready?

Not "is your idea good?" - anyone can have ideas. The real question is whether your group is capable of running a financial system, even a simple one. A timebank, a local currency, a community treasury - these aren't weekend projects. They require sustained coordination, trust, and follow-through.

This isn't about gatekeeping. It's about setting yourself up for success instead of burning out in three months and concluding that "community organizing doesn't work." It does work - but only when the foundations are solid.

The Community Readiness Model, originally developed for public health interventions, identifies nine stages of readiness ranging from "no awareness" to "community ownership." Most communities attempting Web3 initiatives need to be at least at stage 5 - "preparation," where there's active planning and leadership emerging - to have a reasonable chance of sustaining a local node beyond the initial excitement phase.

In Somaliland and across East Africa, communities often have strong existing traditions of collective finance - from hawalas and savings circles to pastoral cooperatives. If your community already practises some form of collective resource management, you may be more ready than you think. The question is whether those existing patterns can extend to new tools.

---

## What Readiness Actually Looks Like

### The People

You need at least **5-7 people who will actually participate**. Not "interested" - actually showing up. Not "might join" - already engaged. Can you name them right now?

Research on cooperative formation consistently shows that groups smaller than 5 struggle with sustainability - there's not enough distributed effort to survive when one person gets sick, moves, or loses interest. Groups larger than 15 in the founding phase tend to fragment into factions before establishing clear governance.

The sweet spot for a founding group is 5-12 committed members who:
- Have shown up to at least 3 previous activities together
- Can explain why they want this (not just "it sounds cool")
- Bring different skills (not 7 developers - you need communicators, organizers, connectors)
- Include at least 2-3 people with experience managing money or projects
- Represent different parts of the community you're trying to serve

### The Need

There must be a **real, shared need** that your community feels:
- "We want to trade skills without money changing hands"
- "We want to support local businesses and keep wealth circulating locally"
- "We want to pool resources for community projects that banks won't fund"
- "We want transparent governance for shared funds"
- "We want an alternative to the remittance fees that eat into money sent to family"

If people can't say why they're here beyond "Web3 is interesting," they won't stay when the work gets tedious - and running a financial system involves a lot of tedious work.

### The Culture

Your group needs a **willingness to try something imperfect**. If people need everything planned before starting, that's a warning sign. The groups that succeed iterate fast and learn by doing. The groups that fail spend six months perfecting bylaws that never get used.

Adrienne Maree Brown calls this "emergent strategy" - the idea that complex systems develop through experimentation, not master plans. Your local node will look different six months after launch than what you planned. That's not failure. That's adaptation.

## Signs Your Community Might Be Ready

### Positive Indicators

- **Track record**: People have worked together on something before - a community garden, a mutual aid effort, a neighbourhood project, a savings circle
- **Existing coordination**: There's already some form of organization (group chats, regular meetups, shared calendar, WhatsApp group)
- **Distributed enthusiasm**: At least 3-4 people are genuinely excited (not just one charismatic leader dragging everyone along)
- **Conflict resilience**: You've dealt with disagreement before and survived. Groups that haven't faced conflict yet are more fragile than groups that have worked through it
- **Trust networks**: There's existing trust between at least some members - people who'd lend each other money without hesitation
- **Skill diversity**: Different people bring different skills - technical, social, financial, organizational
- **Financial collaboration**: You've successfully pooled money for something before, even informally (splitting rent for an event space, group-buying supplies, running a savings circle)

### Warning Signs

- **Solo show**: Only 1-2 people are doing everything while others "support" passively
- **Future tense commitment**: People keep saying "we should do X" but never do. "I'll help when..." is not a commitment - it's a polite decline
- **Decision paralysis**: Every decision requires long debates and consensus processes before anything moves
- **Unresolved conflict**: There's historical tension that hasn't been addressed. This will surface when money enters the picture
- **Tech-first motivation**: People are mostly interested in the technology, not the community outcomes. "I want to learn about DAOs" is not the same as "I want our neighbourhood to thrive"
- **No existing community**: You're trying to build both the community AND the financial system from scratch. Pick one. Build community first.

## The Readiness Test

Try these three tests before launching anything financial:

### Test 1: Organize One Small Thing

Can your group successfully organize one low-stakes, collaborative activity?

Examples:
- A monthly potluck or shared meal where at least 5 people contribute and attend
- A tool library where people actually lend and borrow
- A skill share where 3 or more people teach and 5 or more attend
- A community clean-up that people show up to (not just RSVP to)

**What you're testing**: Can this group coordinate logistics, communicate reliably, and follow through? If you can't organize a shared meal, you cannot organize a financial system.

### Test 2: Handle a Small Conflict

Has your group worked through a disagreement successfully?

Conflict isn't bad - it's inevitable. What matters is whether you can work through it without people storming off, going silent, or forming factions. If your group has never faced a real disagreement, that doesn't mean you're harmonious - it means you haven't been tested yet.

**Create a low-stakes test**: Make a decision where people genuinely disagree (venue for an event, format for a meeting, how to spend a small budget). Watch how the group handles it. Do people listen? Compromise? Steamroll? Go passive? This is a preview of how governance will work when real money is on the line.

### Test 3: Sustain Effort for 3 Months

Have at least 3 people been actively engaged for 3 months in anything together?

New projects always have initial excitement - the "honeymoon phase." Attendance is high, energy is up, everyone's dreaming big. Then real life happens: someone gets busy, someone gets bored, the season changes, a holiday comes.

**The 3-month test** filters for people who show up when it's not exciting. These are your real founding members. If you haven't hit this threshold, keep doing regular activities until you have a proven core group.

## What to Do If You're Not Ready

### Don't Force It

If your community isn't ready, that's okay. It's not a failure - it's information. Launching before you're ready wastes energy and creates bad experiences that make it harder to try again later. Communities have long memories for projects that promised a lot and delivered nothing.

### Build Foundations First

Focus on community building without the complexity of financial systems:

- **Regular meetups** - Even just monthly tea or coffee. Consistency matters more than content. In many East African communities, regular gathering is already a practice - build on what exists.
- **Small collaborative projects** - A community map, a neighbourhood survey, a local directory of skills and resources
- **Trust-building activities** - Shared meals, skill swaps, mutual aid (even small-scale). These build the relational infrastructure your node will run on.
- **Communication infrastructure** - A group chat that people actually use, a shared calendar, a simple newsletter or broadcast list
- **Education** - Learning circles about community economics, cooperative principles, or Web3 basics. The Regen Toolkit itself is designed for this kind of group learning.

### The Local Node Will Wait

A financial system requires trust. You cannot shortcut trust. The technology will still be there in six months. What you're building now - relationships, shared language, patterns of collaboration - is the infrastructure your local node will run on.

The most successful community currency projects in history (Ithaca Hours, Brixton Pound, Sarafu Network in Kenya) all emerged from communities that had been organizing together for years before introducing a financial instrument. The currency was an evolution of existing relationships, not a substitute for them.

The Sarafu Network is particularly instructive: it grew from grassroots community networks in Kenya that had strong existing trust and coordination. The digital community currency worked because the community was already working.

## What to Do If You Are Ready

### Start Small

Even if you're ready, start tiny. The Minimum Viable Node approach applies - don't launch with 47 features. Start with one simple exchange: a timebank with 10 members, a mutual credit system for a specific type of trade, a shared treasury for one specific purpose.

### Document Everything

Write down:
- Who agreed to what responsibilities
- How decisions get made (and by whom)
- What happens if key people leave
- How new members join
- What happens to shared funds if the group dissolves

This doesn't need to be a legal document. A shared document or even a notebook works. But you need it written down before money enters the picture. Verbal agreements are fine for cooking together. They are not fine for managing a treasury.

### Set a Review Date

Pick a date 3 months out where you'll honestly assess: Is this working? What needs to change? Should we continue, pivot, or pause?

Having a built-in review prevents the slow death where nobody wants to be the one to say "this isn't working." It gives everyone permission to be honest.

### Then Launch

Done is better than perfect. Get something running with your core group, then improve it. The best time to polish your governance model is after you've used it for a month and know what actually needs fixing.

## Questions to Ask Your Group

Before starting, sit down together and discuss honestly:

1. **Commitment**: Are we committed to trying this for at least 6 months, even when it's boring or difficult?
2. **Roles**: Who will do what? (Be specific. "We'll all pitch in" is not a plan.)
3. **Resilience**: What's our backup if 2-3 key people leave?
4. **Failure**: How will we handle it if this fails? (This question reveals a lot about group maturity.)
5. **Success**: What does success look like in 6 months? (If people give wildly different answers, you haven't aligned on purpose yet.)
6. **Money**: How much money are we comfortable managing together right now?
7. **Authority**: Who can make decisions without group consensus? About what?

If you can't answer these, you're not ready yet. And that's fine - use these questions as a roadmap for what to work on next.

## Try This

**The Readiness Scorecard.** Before your next group meeting, rate your community on each of the following (1 = not at all, 5 = strongly yes):

- We have 5 or more people who show up consistently: ___
- We've organized something together successfully before: ___
- We've handled a disagreement without it destroying the group: ___
- People can clearly say why they want this, beyond the technology: ___
- We have different skills represented in our group: ___
- We've pooled money or resources together before: ___
- At least 3 people have been engaged for 3 months or more: ___

**Score 25-35:** You're likely ready. Start with a Minimum Viable Node.
**Score 15-24:** You have foundations but gaps. Focus on the lowest-scoring areas for the next 2-3 months.
**Score below 15:** Build community first. Regular gatherings, small projects, trust. The node can wait.

Share the scorecard with your group and discuss honestly. The conversation itself is a readiness indicator - groups that can have this conversation openly are closer to ready than they think.
