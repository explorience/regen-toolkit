## Overview

Web3 represents a shift in how we think about the internet—moving from platforms that own your data to systems where you own your digital life. But it's not a magic solution to everything. Understanding both its strengths and limitations helps you engage meaningfully.

## What Web3 Can Do

### Own Your Digital Assets
Web3 lets you truly own digital items—cryptocurrency, NFTs, tokens representing real-world assets. Unlike buying a song on iTunes (you license it), owning an NFT means you control it. You can sell it, gift it, or hold it—no middleman needed.

### Permissionless Innovation
Anyone can build on public blockchains. No app store approval, no platform taking 30%. This enables **DeFi** (decentralized finance), **DAOs** (decentralized organizations), and new economic models impossible in Web2.

### Transparent Verification
Transactions on blockchains are publicly verifiable. This matters for charity donations (prove funds reach recipients), supply chain tracking (prove ethically sourced), and governance (see how votes were counted).

### Censorship Resistance
Once something is on a public blockchain, it's extremely difficult to remove. This protects free speech in authoritarian contexts and preserves digital history.

## What Web3 Can't Do (Yet)

### Scale Like Traditional Systems
Bitcoin handles ~7 transactions per second; Visa handles ~24,000. Blockchain tech is improving (Layer 2 solutions, new consensus mechanisms) but mass adoption faces real technical hurdles.

### Replace All Databases
Most data doesn't need blockchain's permanence and cost. Your social media posts, emails, or Netflix preferences work fine in traditional databases—adding blockchain adds complexity without benefit.

### Solve All Trust Problems
Blockchains verify data *on-chain*—but what about the real world? If you tokenize a house, someone still needs to legally transfer ownership. **Oracles** (services feeding real-world data to blockchains) help, but the "oracle problem" remains.

### Guarantee Privacy
Public blockchains are... public. Your transactions are visible. While pseudonymous (not tied to your real name by default), sophisticated analysis can often link addresses to identities.

## Practical Examples for Maya

- **Can**: Send money globally without Western Union—borderless, 24/7
- **Can't**: Stream Spotify at scale (blockchain can't handle that volume yet)
- **Can**: Crowdfund a project and guarantee backers receive tokens if funded
- **Can't**: Store your photos privately (on-chain storage is expensive and public)
