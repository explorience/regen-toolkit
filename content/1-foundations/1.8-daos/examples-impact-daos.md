# Real Examples of Impact DAOs

*How communities are using web3 tools to regenerate the world*

---

## What Are Impact DAOs?

If you've ever been part of a permaculture design course, a seed swap, or a neighborhood tool library, you've already experienced something like a DAO in action. A DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization) is simply a group of people who share a mission and make decisions together—with some help from digital tools that make it easier to coordinate.

Impact DAOs take this further. They're groups using these tools specifically to create positive change in the world—whether that's restoring ecosystems, funding local projects, or building new economic systems that work for everyone [Source P].

The key difference from traditional organizations? No central boss. Everyone who joins can have a voice in decisions, and the rules are written into code that everyone can see [Source P]. Think of it like a cooperative that runs itself, where trust is built into the system rather than relying on one person to manage everything.

## Real Examples You Can Understand

### ReFi DAO: Local Roots, Global Reach

ReFi DAO might be the most accessible example for perm. They'reaculture folks building a "regenerative economy" that starts with local communities [Source A].

Imagine if your local permaculture group could connect with similar groups around the world—not just to chat, but to share resources, fund each other's projects, and learn from each other's successes and failures. That's what ReFi DAO does.

They've helped start "Local Nodes" in places like Costa Rica, Barcelona, Tanzania, and Mexico [Source A]. Each node focuses on local environmental challenges:

- **ReFi Costa Rica** merges crypto philanthropy with grassroots activism
- **ReFi Tanzania** addresses urban waste through cleanup systems
- **ReFi BayArea** integrates solar energy with regenerative real estate

What makes this powerful is that these local nodes aren't just isolated projects. They're part of a larger network where ideas, funding, and support flow between them [Source A]. A technique that works in Costa Rica can inspire a project in Tanzania. Money raised in Barcelona can support work in Lagos.

*Sound familiar?* It's exactly how permaculture has spread—through networks of practitioners sharing knowledge locally while staying connected globally.

### Greenpill: Turning "Degens" to "Regens"

Greenpill started with a simple idea: what if people who were already excited about crypto could redirect that energy toward regenerative projects? [Source B]

They call it "Turning Degens to Regens"—moving from speculative betting toward building something that actually helps communities flourish.

Greenpill works through local chapters worldwide. Each chapter organizes grassroots activities in their region, bringing regenerative web3 to local communities [Source B]. They've published books exploring everything from local currency systems to how communities in Kenya manage capital—which connects beautifully to permaculture's emphasis on local economic resilience.

The movement has several participation levels:

- **Beginner**: Learn and connect socially
- **Intermediate**: Share with friends
- **Advanced**: Start an ImpactDAO, organize teachbacks, run regenerative experiments [Source B]

This mirrors how many permaculture teachers got started—one person learns something valuable, then teaches a friend, then organizes a workshop, then eventually starts a full design practice.

### Klima DAO: Carbon Markets for the People

Klima DAO tackles climate change by making carbon offsets more accessible [Source P]. They use a "carbon bridge" (Toucan) that takes verified carbon credits and brings them onto the blockchain where anyone can trade them [Source P].

The vision? Create a transparent, liquid market for carbon credits that anyone can participate in—not just big corporations. Imagine a world where your local land restoration project could earn carbon credits that are automatically tracked, verified, and sellable to anyone, anywhere.

This connects to permaculture's work in a few ways. If you're doing regenerative agriculture or forest restoration, you're sequestering carbon. Right now, capturing that value is complicated and often requires middlemen. Impact DAOs are exploring ways to let communities capture that value directly [Source P].

## Why This Matters for Permaculture

Here's where it gets exciting. Permaculture has always been about:

1. **Working with natural systems** rather than against them
2. **Creating local resilience** while connecting to broader networks
3. **Sharing resources and knowledge** rather than hoarding
4. **Building economies** that regenerate rather than extract

Impact DAOs are exploring how to use technology to do exactly these things—but at scale, and with new tools [Source P].

A DAO can:

- Let a group of land stewards pool money to buy a property together
- Automatically distribute funds to projects based on verified outcomes
- Create transparent systems for sharing resources across continents
- Enable communities to own the infrastructure they depend on [Source A]

None of this replaces the hands-on work of planting food forests or building swales. But it might make it easier to fund that work, coordinate those efforts, and connect with allies around the world [Source B].

## Try This

### 1. Visit a Local Node
Explore the ReFi DAO network at refidao.com. Is there a node near you? Even if there's not, browse what different nodes are doing. Notice how each adapts the same core principles to their local context.

### 2. Imagine Your Own
Think about a project in your community that could benefit from:

- More transparent decision-making
- Easier sharing of resources with groups elsewhere
- New ways to fund regeneration work

You don't need to build it tomorrow. Just notice where the friction is in your current collaboration. What if there were a tool that made that easier?

### 3. Read One Book
Greenpill has published free guides on topics like "Local Regen Guide" and "Ethereum Localism" [Source B]. Pick one that catches your interest. Don't worry about understanding every technical term—just notice what possibilities they see.

### 4. Join a Conversation
Both ReFi DAO and Greenpill have community spaces where newcomers are welcome. You don't need to understand web3 to join—you just need curiosity about how communities are experimenting with new ways to coordinate.

## The Bigger Picture

Impact DAOs aren't a magic solution. They're experiments—some will succeed, others will fail, and everyone is learning. But they're worth watching, and perhaps participating in, because they're asking the same questions permaculture has always asked:

*How do we create systems that regenerate rather than extract? How do we coordinate at scale without losing local wisdom? How do we build economies where everyone benefits?*

The tools are different. The values feel familiar.
