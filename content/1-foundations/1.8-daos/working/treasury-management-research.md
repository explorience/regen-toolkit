## Overview

DAO treasury management refers to the process of managing a decentralized autonomous organization's financial assets—including tokens, stablecoins, and other crypto holdings—to ensure operational sustainability, fund development, and support community growth. [Source P]

The top 50 DAO treasuries (fully vested) are worth approximately $15 billion combined. However, most hold over 90% of their treasuries in native governance tokens, creating significant risk. [Source P]

---

## Why Treasury Diversification Matters

### The Native Token Problem

Native DAO tokens function more like authorized but unissued shares than liquid assets. DAOs cannot easily sell large amounts of their native tokens without causing significant price impact—sometimes over 95% slippage on decentralized exchanges. [Source P]

### The Case for Stablecoins

DAOs should ensure their treasuries hold stablecoins covering at least 2-3 years of operating expenses. This provides several benefits:

1. **De-risking the treasury** — Native tokens are highly volatile; prices can fall 70% or more. Stablecoin holdings enable the DAO to operate from strength during bear markets. [Source P]

2. **Predictable budgeting** — Working groups and grant committees struggle to budget when receiving volatile tokens. Stablecoins simplify financial planning. [Source P]

3. **Contributor payments** — Contributors can receive partial compensation in stablecoins to cover fiat-denominated expenses. [Source P]

4. **Credit and yield** — Treasuries with stablecoins may receive better borrowing terms and can earn yield through lending protocols. [Source P]

## Methods for Treasury Diversification

### 1. Earn Revenue in Stablecoins

The least controversial method. Protocols like Aave use a reserve factor that directs a portion of borrower interest to an ecosystem collector—accruing stablecoins non-dilutively. [Source P]

### 2. Sell Native Tokens for Stablecoins

Several approaches exist:

- **Decentralized exchanges** — Selling on Uniswap, Sushiswap, or aggregators like 1Inch. High slippage risk; can be front-run. [Source P]
- **OTC & market makers** — Wintermute and similar firms can execute large sales with better pricing. Less transparent. [Source P]
- **Batch auctions** — Gnosis Auction matches buyers and sellers at a single clearing price, reducing manipulation risk. [Source P]
- **Bonding curves** — Enable fixed-price token distribution; can be used for native-to-stablecoin swaps. [Source P]

### 3. Strategic Partnerships

DAOs can exchange tokens with long-term holders (VCs, other DAOs) rather than selling on open markets. Partners typically receive tokens at a discount with lock-up periods. Examples include Lido (Paradigm), PoolTogether (ParaFi), and FWB (a16z). [Source P]

UMA Protocol developed **success tokens**—wrapping a DAO's native token with a call option to give VCs upside exposure while providing treasury diversification. [Source P]

### 4. Borrow Against Native Tokens

DAOs can deposit native tokens on lending protocols (Aave, Compound) to borrow stablecoins. Variable rate debt is simpler than partnerships but carries liquidation risk. Fixed-rate options exist through Element and Yield protocols. [Source P]

### 5. Collaborate with Stablecoin Issuers

Protocols like Fei, Frax, and Maker offer mechanisms to accrue stablecoin streams. For example, Tribe's Turbo product lets DAOs borrow FEI after depositing native tokens, then use yield strategies to pay back interest. [Source P]

## DAO Spending and Runway

DAO spending has increased significantly. Major DAOs (Uniswap, Aave, Compound, Index Coop, Yearn, Gitcoin) collectively spend over $100 million annually in native tokens. Despite this, spending remains low relative to optimal growth-stage expenditure. [Source P]

## Governance and Decision-Making

SuperBenefit Knowledge Garden frames DAOs not as static entities but as flows—continuously evolving communities organized around shared resources. Key governance questions include:

- What shared resources does the DAO own?
- How are resources managed?
- How distributed (or concentrated) is executive power? [Source S]

Many communities calling themselves DAOs fall short of true decentralization, often having tokenized structures with multisig treasuries where executive power remains concentrated in a handful of signers. [Source S]

## Maya Protocol: A Case Study

Maya Protocol is a cross-chain liquidity protocol operating as an Automated Market Maker (AMM)—described as "Uniswap, but cross-chain." [Source P]

**Key Statistics:**
- Yearly Volume: ~$748 million
- Yearly Fees Earned: ~$3 million
- Yearly Swaps: 671,000+
- Total Value Locked: ~$9.16 million
- Liquidity Providers: 1,488
- Active Pools: 26

**Governance Token:** CACAO (not MAYA)

**DAO Transition:** Maya Protocol is transitioning to a fully decentralized DAO. This milestone will enable token holders to govern protocol upgrades and treasury allocations. [Source P]

**Technical Approach:**
- Native cross-chain swaps (no wrapping)
- Non-custodial operation
- Permissionless design
- Economic security through liquidity nodes

---

## Key Takeaways

1. DAOs should maintain 2-3 years of runway in stablecoins to survive market downturns.
2. Diversification methods range from earning revenue naturally to borrowing against native tokens.
3. Strategic partnerships help get tokens into long-term holder hands while avoiding market impact.
4. True DAO treasury management requires addressing not just assets but governance structures and power distribution.
5. Maya Protocol exemplifies the evolution toward full DAO governance in cross-chain DeFi.
