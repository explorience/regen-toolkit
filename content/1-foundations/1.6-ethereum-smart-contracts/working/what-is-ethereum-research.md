## Key Concepts

### What is Ethereum?

Ethereum is a decentralized, open-source blockchain that introduced the world to smart contracts. It was first proposed by Vitalik Buterin in 2013 and launched in 2015. [Source P]

"Ethereum is a decentralized network that enables developers to build and deploy smart contracts and decentralized apps. Unlike Bitcoin, which primarily focuses on peer-to-peer digital currency, Ethereum aims to serve as a global, decentralized computer and tokenization platform." [Source P]

Ethereum is the first and most widely-adopted smart contract platform. The network is home to over 4,000 projects and 53 million smart contracts and currently secures over $410 billion in economic value. [Source P]

Ethereum's native currency, ETH, is programmable digital money. It can be used as a medium of exchange, a store of value, and, when staked, as a yield-bearing capital asset. [Source P]

### Smart Contracts

Nick Szabo coined the term "smart contract". In 1994, he wrote an introduction to the concept, and in 1996 he wrote an exploration of what smart contracts could do. Szabo envisioned a digital marketplace where automatic processes enable transactions and business functions to happen without trusted intermediaries. Smart contracts on Ethereum put this vision into practice. [Source S]

"Smart contracts are the fundamental building blocks of Ethereum's application layer. They are computer programs stored on the blockchain that follow 'if this then that' logic, and are guaranteed to execute according to the rules defined by its code, which cannot be changed once created." [Source S]

"The main benefit of a smart contract is that it deterministically executes unambiguous code when certain conditions are met. There is no need to wait for a human to interpret or negotiate the result. This removes the need for trusted intermediaries." [Source S]

### The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM)

"The Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM) is a decentralized virtual environment that executes code consistently and securely across all Ethereum nodes. Nodes run the EVM to execute smart contracts, using 'gas' to measure the computational effort required for operations, ensuring efficient resource allocation and network security." [Source S]

"The EVM behaves as a mathematical function would: Given an input, it produces a deterministic output." [Source S]

Instead of a distributed ledger, Ethereum is a distributed state machine. Ethereum's state is a large data structure which holds not only all accounts and balances, but a machine state, which can change from block to block according to a pre-defined set of rules, and which can execute arbitrary machine code. The specific rules of changing state from block to block are defined by the EVM. [Source S]

## Direct Quotes Worth Including

### On Ethereum's Purpose

"Ethereum functions as an accessible global computer, enabling developers to build open applications and networks, including L2s, via smart contracts. This permissionless platform allows for innovative and varied use cases, extending Ethereum's utility beyond mere financial transactions." [Source P]

### On Smart Contracts as Vending Machines

"A simple metaphor for a smart contract is a vending machine, which works somewhat similarly to a smart contract - specific inputs guarantee predetermined outputs. You select a product, the vending machine displays the price, you pay the price, the vending machine verifies that you paid the right amount, and the vending machine gives you your item. The vending machine will only dispense your desired product after all requirements are met." [Source S]

### On Trustless Execution

"Smart contracts remove the possibility of human interpretation. Instead, smart contracts execute precisely based on the conditions written within the contract's code. This precision means that given the same circumstances, the smart contract will produce the same result." [Source S]

### On the Proof-of-Stake Transition

"In September 2022, Ethereum transitioned from Proof-of-Work (PoW) to Proof-of-Stake (PoS), a consensus mechanism that secures the network through validators who stake ETH. This shift reduced Ethereum's energy consumption by 99% while also increasing network security." [Source P]

## Examples Maya Would Understand

### From Bankless Academy (Use Cases)

**Community Garden Funding / DAO**
- "Decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs) on Ethereum are community-led entities with no central authority, operating through smart contracts. These smart contracts enforce the organization's rules and execute decisions based on the inputs of its members." [Source P]
- Example: Nouns DAO uses its infrastructure to facilitate transparent, collective decision-making around creative and public goods projects. [Source P]

**Crop Insurance**
- Smart contracts can be used for "An insurance policy that pays out automatically." [Source S]

**Tool Lending Library**
- Smart contracts could track: who borrowed what, when it's due back, automatically charge fees if not returned. [Implied from smart contract capabilities]

### From Ethereum.org

**Vending Machine Metaphor**
- "A simple metaphor for a smart contract is a vending machine" - select product, pay price, get item. All requirements must be met or no output. [Source S]

**Child Escrow Example**
- "You could write a smart contract that holds funds in escrow for a child, allowing them to withdraw funds after a specific date. If they try to withdraw before that date, the smart contract won't execute." [Source S]

1. **No specific examples for community gardens or local regenerative projects** - Sources focus on DeFi, NFTs, and mainstream crypto use cases. No mention of how regen communities could use Ethereum.

2. **No guidance on low-technical-setup options** - No mention of whether Maya needs technical knowledge to use any Ethereum-based tools, or if there are user-friendly interfaces.

3. **No discussion of environmental impact concerns addressed** - While the 99% energy reduction is mentioned, there's no discussion of how Ethereum could or couldn't align with regenerative values.

4. **No mention of local/cash-based alternatives** - No discussion of how communities without bank access could use these tools, or alternatives that don't require internet.

5. **No guidance on governance/decision-making for communities** - DAOs are mentioned but not explained in terms a non-technical community organizer would need.

### Technical Gaps

6. **No explanation of gas fees in simple terms** - Gas is mentioned but not explained for a beginner.

7. **No comparison to other chains relevant to regen** - No mention of chains like Celo (mobile-friendly, carbon-negative) or Polygon that might be more accessible.

8. **No mention of what's needed to actually start using Ethereum** - Beyond buying ETH and getting a wallet, no step-by-step guidance for complete beginners.

9. **No discussion of risks, scams, or things to avoid** - No warnings about common pitfalls for newcomers.

10. **No explanation of what "decentralized" actually means practically** - The term is used but not broken down for non-technical readers.
