## Key Concepts (Source-Cited)

### What Are Cooperatives?

- **Cooperatives** are member-owned organisations that pool resources for shared projects [Source S]
- Members make financial contributions (like membership fees) and manage the cooperative together [Source S]
- In agricultural cooperatives, small-scale farmers collectively buy seeds, tools, and land [Source S]
- In Community Supported Agriculture (CSA), members pay farmers upfront for a share of the farm's products each week, providing guaranteed income [Source S]

### Types of Cooperatives

- **Worker cooperatives**: Workers own shares and make decisions about distributing and investing profits (e.g., Mondragon in Spain) [Source S]
- **Consumer cooperatives**: Members pool purchasing power for goods and services [Source S]
- **Community banks**: Like Banco Palmas in Brazil, offering micro-loans and community currencies [Source S]
- **Digital cooperatives**: Member-owned digital networks exploring Web3 models [Source S - KPMG/Orrick Report]

### The Digital Commons

- **Digital commons** are online resources designed and governed by a community with rules concerning access and sharing [Source S]
- They include open-source software, public datasets, educational materials, and platforms for collective knowledge sharing [Source S]
- The digital commons can be managed through blockchain technology and DAOs [Source S]

### Ostrom's Principles Applied to Digital Commons

Elinor Ostrom's eight principles for commons governance can be enhanced through blockchain:

1. **Clearly defined boundaries** — Tokenization creates boundaries for membership and rights [Source S]
2. **Rules fit to local circumstances** — Smart contracts can formalize context-specific rules [Source S]
3. **Participicipatory decision-making** — DAOs enable community voting on governance [Source S]
4. **Self-monitoring** — Blockchain's transparent ledger enables community oversight [Source S]
5. **Graduated sanctions** — Automated enforcement through smart contracts [Source S]
6. **Conflict resolution** — On-chain arbitration mechanisms [Source S]
7. **Rights to organize** — Decentralized governance without central authority interference [Source S]
8. **Nested governance** — Multi-layer coordination from local to global [Source S]

## Examples/Case Studies

### Traditional Cooperatives

1. **Mondragon (Spain)** - Federation of worker cooperatives across manufacturing, retail, finance, and education. Workers own shares and decide on profit distribution, including funding renewable energy and community education projects [Source S]

2. **Banco Palmas (Brazil)** - Community bank founded in 1998 in Conjunto Palmeiras. Offered micro-loans and introduced the Palma community currency to keep money circulating locally. Transformed a poor neighborhood into a vibrant local economy [Source S]

3. **Magrana Vallesana (Spain)** - Food cooperative that used debt-based crowdsourcing to fund a cold room [Source S]

4. **Community Supported Agriculture (CSA)** - Members pay farmers upfront for weekly shares, providing guaranteed income and sharing risks and rewards [Source S]

### Digital Commons & Web3 Examples

1. **MakerDAO** - Lending protocol governed by MKR token holders who vote on stability fees, collateral requirements, and risk management strategies [Source S]

2. **MolochDAO** - DAO that collectively funds Ethereum ecosystem public goods projects [Source S]

3. **KlimaDAO** - DAO experiment to align decentralized finance with climate/sustainability goals [Source S]

4. **Regen Network** - Blockchain platform focused on ecological regeneration and land management [Source S]

5. **Greenpill Chapters** - Decentralized network of local groups with shared regenerative principles [Source B]

## Financing the Commons

From the research, key financing strategies for commons [Source S]:

- **Crowdfunding** - Donation-based, reward-based, equity-based, or debt-based
- **Cooperatives** - Member-owned pooling of resources
- **Community currencies** - Local exchange mediums (like Palma) that keep wealth circulating locally
- **State support** - Grants, tax breaks, commons-public partnerships
- **Philanthropy** - Long-term grants from foundations
- **Bioregional Financing Facilities (BFFs)** - Fund local grassroots ecological projects

## Key Principles for Grounded Regen (Maya's Context)

Based on sources, cooperative economics and digital commons offer regen practitioners:

1. **Member ownership** - Communities own and control their economic systems
2. **Democratic governance** - One member, one vote regardless of financial contribution
3. **Local wealth circulation** - Community currencies keep resources local
4. **Shared risk and reward** - Cooperative members share both costs and benefits
5. **Multi-capital approach** - Recognizing financial, social, cultural, material, living, spiritual, experiential, and intellectual capital [Source B]
6. **Bridging global and local** - Cosmo-local coordination with global standards, local implementation
7. **Transparent governance** - Blockchain can enable open, auditable decision-making
