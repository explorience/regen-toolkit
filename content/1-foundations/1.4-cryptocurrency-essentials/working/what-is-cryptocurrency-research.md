## Key Concepts:

### What is Cryptocurrency?
Cryptocurrency is a digital payment system that doesn't rely on banks to verify transactions. It's a peer-to-peer system that allows anyone to send and receive payments anywhere. Instead of physical money, cryptocurrency payments are digital entries in an online database recording specific transactions [Source S - Kaspersky]. Cryptography is used to secure these transactions, involving advanced coding for storing and transmitting data between wallets and public ledgers, aiming for security and safety [Source S - Kaspersky]. The first cryptocurrency was Bitcoin, founded in 2009 [Source S - Kaspersky].

### Digital Money
Cryptocurrency is a form of digital money that exists digitally or virtually [Source S - Kaspersky]. It's designed to electronically mimic features of a cash transaction, allowing peer-to-peer transactions without needing to know or trust the other person, and without a central party like a bank [Source S - RBA, previously fetched search snippet]. The idea of "digital cash" isn't new; credit cards and services like PayPal also allow electronic transactions [Source S - Charles Schwab, previously fetched search snippet].

### Tokens
"Digital assets" is a broad term encompassing anything minted and exchanged on a blockchain [Source S - PwC]. Crypto tokens represent digital assets or interests built on existing blockchains, facilitating transactions and project funding [Source S - Investopedia, previously fetched search snippet]. Unlike cryptocurrencies, which are native to their blockchains, tokens are created through processes like Initial Coin Offerings (ICOs) [Source S - Investopedia, previously fetched search snippet]. Digital tokens are a specific type of digital asset built on existing blockchains and represent utility, ownership, or value within a particular ecosystem or project [Source S - Fiat Republic, previously fetched search snippet].

## Examples Maya Would Understand:

*   **Cryptocurrency as Digital Cash:** Imagine sending money to a friend through an app like PayPal, but instead of a bank handling the transfer, a network of computers worldwide verifies and records the transaction. This network acts like a public, unchangeable ledger, ensuring everyone agrees on who owns what, without a central authority [Source S - Kaspersky, RBA].
*   **Blockchain as a Community Ledger:** Think of a shared community ledger, like a record book for a permaculture collective. Every time someone adds a new entry (a transaction, a new planting record, etc.), everyone in the community gets an updated copy. Once an entry is made and agreed upon by the community, it's very difficult to change, ensuring transparency and trust within the group. If one person's copy gets damaged, others still have the correct record [Source S - Bitdegree, PwC].
*   **Tokens for Specific Purposes:** In a permaculture project, imagine you have "seed tokens" that represent ownership of a certain type of seed, or "tool tokens" that give you access to shared tools. These tokens exist digitally and can be exchanged within the project for specific resources or privileges, managed by the project's rules rather than a bank [Source S - Fiat Republic, Investopedia].
*   **Decentralization (no central authority):** Picture a local farmers' market where no single person or organization controls all the prices or what can be sold. Instead, the community of farmers and buyers collectively agrees on the rules and transactions. This is similar to how decentralized cryptocurrencies operate â€“ no single bank or government is in charge [Source S - Kaspersky, Bitdegree].
