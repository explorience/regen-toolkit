## Key Concepts (Source-Cited)

### What Are Stablecoins?
- Stablecoins are the blockchain equivalent of fiat currency, like the dollar or euro. [Source P]
- Stablecoins are ERC-20 tokens that allow DeFi users to quickly move between fiat value and crypto value while staying on the blockchain. [Source P]
- Stablecoins have become a cornerstone of the DeFi ecosystem, reaching over $140 billion USD in supply value at their 2022 peak. [Source P]
- Stablecoins settled over $7 trillion in transaction value in 2022 â€” that's ~0.3% of global USD settlement in 2022. [Source P]

### Why Hold Stablecoins?
- **Stability:** Holding stablecoins in your self-custody wallet is like holding fiat currency, but on the blockchain. When holding a stablecoin like USDC, you can expect it to maintain 1:1 value with the U.S. dollar while the prices of assets like ether and bitcoin fluctuate. [Source P]
- **Flexibility:** Because this pegged value exists as an ERC-20 token on the blockchain, it's easy to move between fiat value and crypto value. [Source P]
- **Access:** Stablecoins provide access to a range of decentralized financial services, like permissionless borrowing or lending to earn interest. [Source P]
- **Security:** Cryptography makes it extremely difficult for attackers to capture or forge transactions. [Source P]

### Three Categories of Stablecoins

#### 1. Fiat-backed Stablecoins
- 1:1 collateralized by real-world fiat reserves. [Source P]
- Notable examples: Tether (USDT) and Circle (USDC). [Source P]
- Revenue generation: investing reserves in short-term US Treasuries, collecting transaction fees, offering lending services. [Source P]
- **Innovation - Glo Dollar:** The Glo Foundation created USDGLO, a US dollar-backed stablecoin that directs interest earned from reserves towards basic income programs for people in extreme poverty. [Source P]
- **Considerations:**
  - Collateral Audits: Holders need assurance that stablecoin tokens are matched one-to-one by fiat reserves. USDC regularly undergoes independent audits; USDT has historically made it difficult to know the total value of their locked assets. [Source P]
  - Censorship Risk: Both USDC and USDT have freeze functions that can lock on-chain holdings in cases of disagreeable activity, even for non-custodial wallets. [Source P]

#### 2. Crypto-collateralized Stablecoins
- Overcollateralized by crypto deposits into DeFi protocols (sometimes up to 200%). [Source P]
- All collateralized assets are viewable on-chain, giving users 24/7 access to the real composition. [Source P]
- Notable example: MakerDAO's Dai (DAI). [Source P]
- DAI is backed by BTC, ETH, stablecoins, real world assets, and several other components. [Source P]
- **Considerations:**
  - Collateral Valuation: To mitigate risks of diverse assets, DAI is overcollateralized. Even if ETH price crashes by 20%, DAI would still have enough collateral to cover tokens. [Source P]
  - Counterparty Risk: Reliance on multiple asset classes means higher chance one asset will experience difficulty. [Source P]
  - Governance Risk: Managed by decentralized governance voters; risks of human error or governance capture. [Source P]

#### 3. Algorithmic Stablecoins
- Fixed supply/demand algorithms that maintain price stability. [Source P]
- Algorithm reduces tokens in circulation when price falls below peg, injects new tokens when price surpasses peg. [Source P]
- Both the algorithm and smart contracts are public, meaning anyone can audit or clone. [Source P]
- Nearly eliminate counterparty risk since they have fewest external dependencies. [Source P]
- Notable examples: Liquity (LUSD) and Frax (FRAX). [Source P]
- **Considerations:**
  - Dynamic Holdings: Token supply adjustments mean your number of tokens will fluctuate. Total value remains constant, but this can confuse new users. [Source P]
  - Highly Technical: You need to understand the technology to build confidence. [Source P]
  - Emergent Tech Risk: Unknown smart contract risks could be present. Only use algorithmic stablecoins with several smart contract audits. [Source P]

### Choosing a Stablecoin
- Depends on your needs, values, and risk tolerance. [Source P]
- **Fiat-backed:** Values: Conventionality, institutional trust. Risks: Opaque collateral backing, ability for provider to freeze funds. [Source P]
- **Crypto-collateralized:** Values: Diversification, transparency, progression. Risks: Crypto market volatility, dependence on other assets. [Source P]
- **Algorithmic:** Values: Self-sovereignty, transparency, progression. Risks: Higher risk of smart contract bugs. [Source P]

### Where to Buy Stablecoins
- Centralized Exchanges (CEXs) offer popular fiat-backed stablecoins. [Source P]
- Decentralized Exchanges (DEXs) or wallet on-ramp services like MetaMask Buy for crypto-collateralized and algorithmic tokens. [Source P]

### Earning Interest on Stablecoins
- Some CEXs offer yield by holding stablecoins, funded by platform profits. [Source]
- DeFi lending platforms connect lenders and borrowers, managing risk through on-chain collateral and smart contracts. [Source P]
- Stablecoin lenders can earn annual returns far higher than traditional banking â€” but where there's reward, there's risk. [Source P]
- Platforms to research: Aave.com, Curve.fi [Source P]

### What Happens If a Stablecoin Loses Its Peg?
- Price can shift slightly (usually around +/- 2%) in peak network hours. This effect is usually temporary, with prices stabilized by traders arbitrage. [Source P]
- Cases where stablecoins lose peg beyond safe ranges: USDC (March 2023 - temporary), Terra (May 2022 - permanent failure). [Source P]
- Some fiat-backed issuers like USDC offer 1:1 redemption from stablecoin to fiat through their website. [Source P]
