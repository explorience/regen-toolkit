# Funding Your Gatherings

*Practical strategies for funding community events — from zero budget to grant-funded*

---

## The Most Important Principle

Most community gatherings don't need money. They need people, a space, and a purpose. If you're waiting until you have funding to start gathering, you'll never start.

The best community events in the world are potlucks, park meetups, and living room conversations that cost nothing. Money helps with scale and comfort, but it's never the prerequisite. Your community's most valuable resources are time, skills, and goodwill — none of which require a budget line.

That said, as your local node grows, you'll want to host events that require some investment: workshops with materials, larger gatherings with food, events in proper venues, or conferences with speakers. Here's how to fund them at every level.

---

## Tier 1: Zero Budget ($0)

These formats cost nothing and can be remarkably effective:

### Potluck Gatherings
Everyone brings something to share. Potlucks distribute both cost and effort, and the act of bringing food creates psychological investment in the gathering. People who bring something feel more ownership than people who show up to a catered event.

**Tips for great potlucks:**
- Use a shared sign-up sheet (Google Sheet or Signal thread) so you don't get 15 bags of chips
- Always have one backup person committed to bringing a main dish in case sign-ups are sparse
- Dietary accommodations: ask people to label their dishes and note allergens
- Paper plates are fine. This isn't a dinner party — it's community building.

### Free Venue Strategies
- **Public libraries**: Most have free meeting rooms you can reserve. Some have projectors and whiteboards.
- **Parks and public spaces**: Weather permitting, outdoor gatherings are free and naturally casual. Bring a blanket, find a picnic table.
- **Member homes**: Living room meetups create intimacy. Rotate hosts to share the load.
- **Partner organizations**: Co-working spaces, community centers, churches, and nonprofits often have meeting space they'll share for free if your mission aligns with theirs.
- **Cafés and pubs**: Many have back rooms or off-peak hours where they'll let groups meet if members buy drinks. This costs each person a few dollars but costs the organizer nothing.
- **University spaces**: If any members are students or faculty, campus rooms can often be reserved for community use.

### Skill Swaps
Instead of hiring someone to facilitate, teach, or provide food — find community members who can contribute their skills. A member who's a professional facilitator can run your governance workshop. A member who bakes can bring bread. A member with a projector can bring AV equipment.

This approach has a double benefit: it costs nothing AND it deepens members' investment in the community.

## Tier 2: Low Budget ($10-100)

When you need a little money but not much:

### Member Contributions

**Sliding scale**: Suggest $5-15 per person, but make it genuinely optional with a "pay what you can" model. Never turn anyone away for lack of funds. The language matters: "Suggested contribution: $10. All are welcome regardless of ability to pay."

**Pass the hat**: At the event itself, pass around a jar or set up a QR code for mobile payments. Low pressure, people give what feels right. This typically raises $50-100 at a 15-20 person event.

**Monthly dues**: If your node has a regular gathering schedule, small monthly dues ($5-10/month) create a predictable event fund. With 20 members at $5/month, that's $100/month — enough for most community gatherings.

### What $50-100 Buys
- Pizza and drinks for 15-20 people
- Printed materials for a workshop
- A basic venue rental for 2-3 hours
- Supplies for a hands-on activity (seed kits for a garden workshop, craft supplies for a community art project)
- A small honorarium for a local speaker

### Crypto-Native Collection
Use a simple multisig wallet or a platform like Juicebox to collect small contributions from members. This serves double duty: it funds the event AND gives members practice using Web3 tools. Even collecting $5 in USDC from each member is a learning moment.

## Tier 3: Medium Budget ($100-500)

For larger events, workshops with materials, or events requiring proper venues:

### Local Business Sponsorship

Local businesses often sponsor community events in exchange for visibility. This works best when there's genuine alignment — a local café sponsoring a community dinner, a co-working space hosting a workshop, a bookshop providing a reading space.

**What to offer sponsors:**
- Logo on event materials and social posts
- Public thank-you during the event
- Table or display at the event
- Introduction to your community members (potential customers)
- Photos of their logo/space in use (social proof for them)

**How to approach:**
- Start with businesses you already patronize — warm connections convert better
- Be specific about what you need: "$200 for food for 40 people" is better than "sponsorship"
- Offer something concrete in return, not vague "exposure"
- Follow through: actually tag them, share photos, write a genuine thank-you

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't approach businesses you've never visited
- Don't promise things you can't deliver (attendance numbers you're guessing at)
- Don't make it transactional — frame it as mutual community investment

### Small Grants

Several organizations offer small grants ($100-500) for community events:

- **Local community foundations**: Most cities have foundations that fund community activities. Applications are usually simple.
- **Municipal arts/culture grants**: City governments often have small grants for community gatherings, especially if there's an educational or cultural component.
- **Cooperative development funds**: If your node has a cooperative structure, development funds often support member education and community outreach.

### Web3-Native Funding

- **Gitcoin Grants**: If your local node has a Gitcoin profile, you can run a round specifically for community events. Quadratic funding amplifies small contributions — even $1 donations count.
- **Giveth**: List your event on Giveth to receive crypto donations from the broader regen community.
- **DAO treasury**: If your node has a treasury, allocating a portion to community events is one of the highest-return investments you can make. Events build the social infrastructure that everything else depends on.

## Tier 4: Large Budget ($500+)

For conferences, multi-day events, or significant community gatherings:

### Conference-Style Funding

For events with 50+ attendees, you'll likely need $1,000-5,000+. This requires more structured fundraising:

- **Ticket sales**: Even small fees ($10-25) add up. Always offer free/reduced tickets for those who can't pay.
- **Multiple sponsors**: Tier your sponsorship (Gold/Silver/Bronze) with different levels of visibility.
- **Speaker budgets**: If you're bringing in speakers from outside your community, budget for travel and honoraria ($200-500 per speaker is standard for community events).
- **Grants**: Apply 3-6 months in advance. Ethereum Foundation, Protocol Labs, and various Web3 ecosystem grants fund community education events.

### Budget Template for a 50-Person Event

| Item | Low | Medium | High |
|------|-----|--------|------|
| Venue (4 hours) | $0 (donated) | $200 | $500 |
| Food & drinks | $200 (pizza) | $400 (catered) | $800 (full service) |
| Materials/printing | $50 | $100 | $200 |
| Speaker honoraria | $0 (community) | $200 | $500 |
| AV/tech | $0 (borrowed) | $100 | $300 |
| Misc/contingency | $50 | $100 | $200 |
| **Total** | **$300** | **$1,100** | **$2,500** |

## Financial Transparency

Whatever your budget level, be transparent about money. Community trust erodes fast when finances are opaque.

**Best practices:**
- Share your budget publicly before the event (what you plan to spend)
- Share actual expenses after the event (what you actually spent)
- If you have surplus funds, decide together what to do with them
- Use transparent tools: a shared spreadsheet, an on-chain treasury, or at minimum a public accounting
- Never let one person handle money without accountability

---

## The Sustainability Question

The real challenge isn't funding one event — it's creating a sustainable funding model for ongoing gatherings.

**Models that work long-term:**
- **Membership dues** fund regular events predictably
- **Rotating sponsors** spread the ask across multiple businesses
- **Treasury allocation** dedicates a percentage of node funds to community events
- **Hybrid model**: Free regular events (potlucks, park meetups) + funded quarterly events (workshops, celebrations)

The key insight: don't over-spend on events early. Communities that start with expensive, polished events struggle when the budget runs out. Communities that start with free potlucks and gradually introduce funded events as they grow build sustainable rhythms.

---

## Moving Forward

Start free. Gather your community with zero budget and see what emerges. When you need money, start with member contributions. When you need more, explore sponsorship and grants. Scale your budget with your community's growth, not ahead of it.

The most important thing isn't the catering or the venue — it's whether people leave feeling more connected than when they arrived. That costs nothing.
