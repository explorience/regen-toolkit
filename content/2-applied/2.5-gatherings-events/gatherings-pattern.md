# The Gatherings Pattern

*A framework for designing gatherings that build community, deepen relationships, and create lasting impact*

---

## Beyond "Let's Have a Meetup"

Most community gatherings follow a familiar script: find a venue, set a time, post an invite, hope people show up, give a presentation, do Q&A, go home. This format works for information transfer. It fails at everything else.

The gatherings pattern is a different approach — one that treats every gathering as an intentional design challenge. As Priya Parker argues in *The Art of Gathering*, "gatherings crackle and flourish when real thought goes into them, when (often invisible) structure is baked into them, and when a host has the curiosity, willingness, and generosity of spirit to try."

For local nodes, gatherings aren't just events — they're the primary mechanism for building the trust, relationships, and shared understanding that community finance systems require. A well-designed gathering can accomplish in two hours what months of online messaging cannot.

---

## The Pattern: Purpose → People → Place → Process → Practice

### 1. Purpose: Why Are We Gathering?

Every gathering needs a specific, articulable purpose. Not "to learn about Web3" — that's a topic, not a purpose. A purpose answers: what will be different after this gathering? What transformation are we creating?

**Strong purposes:**
- "To help 10 community members set up their first wallet and make their first transaction"
- "To collectively decide how to allocate our community treasury this quarter"
- "To surface and address tensions in our governance process"
- "To introduce 5 new people to 5 existing members through meaningful conversation"

**Weak purposes:**
- "To talk about blockchain"
- "To hang out"
- "To network"
- "Monthly meetup"

The purpose shapes every subsequent decision. If your purpose is trust-building, you need small groups and vulnerable conversation — not a lecture hall. If your purpose is skill-building, you need hands-on practice — not slides.

### 2. People: Who Needs to Be There?

The guest list is a design choice, not a default. Who you invite determines the dynamics, the conversations, and the outcomes.

**Key questions:**
- Who absolutely must be there for the purpose to be achieved?
- Who would add unexpected value or perspective?
- Who might dominate or derail? (You can still invite them — but design around it)
- What's the right group size for this purpose?

**Group size guidelines:**
- **4-6 people**: Deep conversation, vulnerability, decision-making
- **8-15 people**: Workshop, skill-building, collaborative design
- **20-40 people**: Community meeting, diverse perspectives, energy
- **50+**: Celebration, inspiration, movement-building (but intimacy is lost)

Don't default to "everyone's invited." Sometimes the most powerful gatherings are small, curated, and intentional.

### 3. Place: Where Does This Happen?

The space shapes the experience more than most organizers realize. A circle of chairs creates different energy than rows facing a stage. A living room creates different intimacy than a conference room.

**Space principles:**
- **Match space to purpose**: Vulnerable conversations need comfortable, private spaces. Energetic workshops need room to move. Decision-making needs everyone able to see each other.
- **Arrive early, arrange the room**: Don't accept the default setup. Move furniture. Create the configuration that serves your purpose.
- **Remove barriers**: If people need to be equals, don't have some sitting and some standing. If people need to connect, don't put tables between them.
- **Consider accessibility**: Can everyone physically get there? Is it wheelchair-accessible? Near public transit? Affordable to reach?

**Outdoor gatherings** work beautifully for trust-building and creative thinking. Nature reduces stress hormones, encourages openness, and breaks the association with formal meetings. Community gardens, parks, and walking paths can be powerful gathering spaces.

### 4. Process: What Happens and When?

This is where most gatherings succeed or fail. Good process creates the conditions for the purpose to be achieved. Bad process — or no process — produces meandering conversations that frustrate participants.

**The Arc of a Gathering:**

**Opening (10-15 minutes)**
- Welcome and land the purpose: "We're here because..."
- Acknowledge the space and context
- Set agreements (not "ground rules" — agreements are mutual)
- Create connection early: a round of names + one personal question

**Body (main activity, 60-90 minutes)**
- Structured activities that serve the purpose
- Alternate between small group and full group
- Build in movement — sitting for 2 hours kills energy
- Create moments of surprise or delight

**Closing (10-15 minutes)**
- Harvest: What are we taking away?
- Commitments: What are we each doing next?
- Appreciation: What worked? Who contributed?
- Preview: What's coming next?

**Process formats that work for local nodes:**

- **World Café**: Rotating small-group conversations around key questions. Great for surfacing diverse perspectives on complex issues. Each round, one person stays as "host" while others rotate.
- **Fishbowl**: Inner circle discusses while outer circle listens. Anyone can tap in. Creates focused dialogue with audience engagement.
- **Open Space Technology**: Participants set the agenda. Whoever shows up is the right people. Whatever happens is the only thing that could have. Great for large groups with diverse interests.
- **Liberating Structures**: A collection of 33 microstructures that replace traditional presentations and managed discussions. Designed specifically to include everyone and unleash collective intelligence.
- **Circle Practice**: Sitting in circle, using a talking piece. Everyone speaks, everyone listens. Powerful for difficult conversations and decision-making.

### 5. Practice: What Happens After?

A gathering isn't complete when people leave — it's complete when the purpose has been fulfilled, and that usually requires follow-through.

**Immediate (within 24 hours):**
- Send a personal thank-you to each participant
- Share photos, notes, or outputs from the gathering
- Follow up on any commitments made

**Short-term (within a week):**
- Connect people who should talk further
- Share resources mentioned during the gathering
- Document decisions and action items
- Collect feedback (brief survey or personal check-in)

**Long-term (ongoing):**
- Track commitments and follow through
- Reference the gathering in future communications
- Build on what emerged — don't let insights die
- Use what you learned to design the next gathering better

## Common Patterns for Local Nodes

### The Onboarding Gathering

**Purpose**: Help 5-10 new people understand what your local node does and make their first contribution.

**Format**: 2 hours. Start with food and informal conversation (30 min). Short overview of the node — not a lecture, a conversation (15 min). Hands-on activity: set up a wallet, make a first transaction, contribute to a decision (45 min). Small group discussion: what excites you? what worries you? (20 min). Close with next steps and how to stay connected (10 min).

### The Governance Gathering

**Purpose**: Make a specific decision together — how to allocate funds, change a policy, onboard new partners.

**Format**: 90 minutes. Check-in round (10 min). Present the decision clearly — what exactly are we deciding? (10 min). Small groups explore options (20 min). Full group discussion using consent-based process (30 min). Decision and commitment (10 min). Close (10 min).

### The Trust-Building Gathering

**Purpose**: Deepen relationships between existing members.

**Format**: Evening gathering, 2.5 hours. Shared meal (45 min). Structured conversation: pairs share something personal using a prompt like "What's something you're carrying that you don't usually talk about?" (30 min). Full circle: what did you hear that resonated? (20 min). Collaborative activity: cook together, make art, play a game (30 min). Close with appreciations (15 min).

### The Learning Gathering

**Purpose**: Build specific skills — wallet setup, proposal writing, facilitation, conflict resolution.

**Format**: 2 hours. Brief context (10 min). Demonstration (15 min). Hands-on practice in pairs or small groups (45 min). Troubleshooting and Q&A (20 min). Reflection: what clicked? what's still confusing? (15 min). Resources and next steps (15 min).

## Principles That Make It Work

**1. Generous authority.** Someone needs to host — to make decisions about format, timing, and flow. This isn't about control; it's about creating the conditions where others can fully participate without worrying about logistics.

**2. Start with connection before content.** People need to feel seen before they can think together. Even 5 minutes of personal check-in transforms the quality of everything that follows.

**3. Design for the quiet ones.** The loudest voices don't have the best ideas. Use pair conversations, written reflections, and small groups to create space for introverts and those who process slowly.

**4. Honor endings.** How you close matters as much as how you open. A rushed ending leaves people feeling incomplete. A thoughtful closing crystallizes meaning and creates momentum.

**5. Iterate.** Every gathering is an experiment. Debrief afterward: what worked? What didn't? What would we change? The best gatherers are perpetual learners.

---

## Moving Forward

The gatherings pattern isn't a rigid formula — it's a set of lenses for designing intentional experiences. Use what serves your community and discard what doesn't.

Start with your next gathering. Pick one element to be more intentional about — maybe it's the opening, maybe it's the guest list, maybe it's the follow-up. See what shifts. Then iterate.

The communities that thrive aren't the ones with the best technology or the most funding. They're the ones that gather well — that create spaces where people feel seen, heard, and inspired to contribute. Everything else follows from that.
