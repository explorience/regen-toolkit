# Inclusive Practices for Community Finance

*How to build community finance systems that welcome everyone*

---

## Why Inclusion Matters

Community finance is supposed to be for everyone. That's the point — alternatives to extractive financial systems, built by and for communities. But too often, community finance ends up serving the same people who are already served well: those with technical knowledge, social capital, financial cushion, and time.

Exclusion happens not because communities are mean — but because default designs exclude. The tech-heavy onboarding process excludes those without technical skills. The evening meetings exclude those working night shifts. The jargon-filled discussions exclude those new to the space. The informal networks exclude those outside the existing community.

Intentional inclusion isn't just ethical — it's practical. A community finance system that only serves the already-privileged isn't regenerative. It's just a smaller, more niche version of the extractive systems it's trying to replace.

---

## Dimensions of Inclusion

Inclusion isn't one thing. It operates across multiple dimensions:

### Economic Inclusion
Can people with limited financial resources participate?

- **Low or no entry barriers**: No minimum deposit, no membership fees (or sliding scale)
- **Alternative contributions accepted**: Time, skills, care work — not just money
- **Small transaction minimums**: Allow 1-hour timebank trades, not just large exchanges
- **Access to resources**: If you have a community fund, can people access it?

### Technical Inclusion
Can people with limited technical knowledge participate?

- **Low-tech options**: Not everyone can use a wallet. Can they participate through a trusted friend, a simplified interface, or paper?
- **Jargon-free communication**: Explain terms. Don't assume knowledge.
- **Patient support**: Some people need more help. Provide it without condescension.

### Temporal Inclusion
Can people with limited time participate?

- **Flexible meeting times**: Rotate between morning, afternoon, evening, weekend
- **Async participation**: Not everyone can attend meetings. Allow input through written proposals, Signal threads, or recorded updates
- **Variable commitment levels**: Not everyone can be core members. Create meaningful roles for casual participants

### Social Inclusion
Can people from different backgrounds participate?

- **Neutral spaces**: Not everyone is comfortable in bars, churches, or homes. Choose accessible public spaces
- **Culturally appropriate events**: Food, language, and norms matter
- **Explicitly welcoming**: Don't assume people know they're welcome. Tell them

### Accessibility
Can people with disabilities participate?

- **Physical accessibility**: Wheelchair access, hearing loops, sensory-friendly spaces
- **Cognitive accessibility**: Clear language, written materials, processing time
- **Neurodiversity support**: Quiet spaces, written agendas, permission to step out

## Practical Inclusion Strategies

### 1. Start with Language

Words signal who's included and who's not.

**Do:**
- Use plain language in all communications
- Define technical terms when you must use them
- Offer translations if your community is multilingual
- Ask: "Is this clear?" and mean it

**Don't:**
- Use jargon without explanation ("rekt," "gas," "rug pull," " DAO")
- Assume everyone knows the context
- Talk over people's heads
- Dismiss questions as "basic"

### 2. Design Multiple On-Ramps

Not everyone enters the same way. Create options:

- **In-person onboarding**: For those who prefer face-to-face
- **Digital onboarding**: For those comfortable online
- **Buddy system**: Pair newcomers with existing members
- **Graduated engagement**: Start with low-commitment participation, increase over time

### 3. Rotate What Gets Centered

Default practices center whoever designed them. Rotate:

- **Meeting times**: Morning/afternoon/evening/weekend
- **Event types**: Social/educational/working/celebratory
- **Decision spaces**: Formal meetings/informal conversations/written input
- **Leadership roles**: Different people facilitate, organize, represent

### 4. Explicitly Invite

Don't assume people know they're welcome. Say it directly:

- "This is for anyone interested in local exchange"
- "No experience necessary"
- "All are welcome regardless of background"
- "We value diverse perspectives"

And mean it. Inclusion requires intention, not just words.

### 5. Create Accountability

Make inclusion visible:

- Track who's participating and who's not
- Ask about barriers directly: "What's preventing you from participating more?"
- Address patterns: if certain groups are consistently absent, investigate why
- Name inclusion as a value and measure it

## Common Inclusion Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Inclusion as Tokenism
Inviting diverse people but not changing power structures. True inclusion means sharing power, not just having diverse faces in the room.

### Mistake 2: One-Size-Fits-All Design
Designing for a hypothetical "average" person who doesn't exist. Multiple options beat single defaults.

### Mistake 3: Burden on Marginalized Groups
Expecting excluded people to educate others, do inclusion work, or represent their entire group. This is labor. Compensate it.

### Mistake 4: Accessibility as Afterthought
Retrofitting accessibility is expensive and incomplete. Design inclusively from the start.

### Mistake 5: Measuring Participation, Not Belonging
Counting heads isn't enough. Do people feel valued? Do they contribute? Do they stay?

## Inclusion in Community Finance Specifically

Community finance has specific inclusion challenges:

### Challenge: Financial Literacy
Not everyone understands economics, tokens, or accounting.

**Solutions:**
- Financial education as a core offering
- Paired transactions: someone walks them through first trades
- Simple interfaces that hide complexity

### Challenge: Technical Barriers
Wallets, keys, blockchain can be intimidating.

**Solutions:**
- Custodial options for those who can't manage keys
- Trusted intermediaries who can help
- Low-tech alternatives (paper ledgers, verbal agreements for small transactions)

### Challenge: Time Poverty
Those most excluded from traditional finance often work multiple jobs.

**Solutions:**
- Asynchronous participation options
- Value time contributions equally to money
- Low-time-commitment roles for those with less availability

### Challenge: Trust Barriers
Those harmed by extractive systems may distrust any financial system.

**Solutions:**
- Start with small, bounded interactions
- Build relationships before transactions
- Be transparent about how the system works
- Acknowledge historical harms

## Creating an Inclusive Culture

Beyond practices, culture matters:

- **Name exclusion when you see it**: "I noticed we just used a lot of jargon. Let me translate."
- **Welcome questions**: No question is too basic
- **Correct condescension**: "That's not a dumb question. It's a good one."
- **Celebrate diversity**: Different perspectives make the community stronger
- **Iterate**: Inclusion is a practice, not a destination

---

## Measuring Inclusion

Ask:
- Who's participating? (Demographics)
- Who's not? (Who's missing?)
- What barriers exist? (Ask directly)
- Do people feel valued? (Survey)
- Do diverse voices influence decisions? (Observation)

---

## Moving Forward

Inclusion isn't a box to check — it's an ongoing practice. Start by asking: who isn't here that should be? What barriers might they face? What would make this space work for them?

Then act on what you learn.
