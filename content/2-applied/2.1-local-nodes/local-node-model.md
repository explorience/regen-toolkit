# Local Node Models

*Choosing the right organizational model for your community*

---

## Why Model Matters

How you organize determines what you can accomplish. A flat collective works for 8 people planning events. It doesn't work for 80 people managing a treasury. Choosing the right model for your stage and purpose is one of the most important early decisions.

---

## Model 1: The Collective

### How It Works
Everyone is equal. Decisions by consensus. No formal roles. Open membership.

### Best For
- Very small groups (5-10 people)
- Early-stage exploration
- Low-stakes activities (meetups, skill shares)

### Strengths
- Maximum equality
- Simple
- Low barrier to participation

### Limitations
- Decision-making gets slow as group grows
- No accountability for tasks
- Relies on goodwill, not structure
- Vulnerable to dominant personalities

---

## Model 2: The Working Group Model

### How It Works
The community is organized into working groups (events, treasury, onboarding, communications). Each group has autonomy within its domain. A coordination layer (weekly sync, shared docs) connects them.

### Best For
- Medium groups (10-30 people)
- Active nodes with multiple functions
- Communities with diverse skills

### Strengths
- Distributed workload
- Domain expertise develops
- People contribute where they're skilled
- Scales better than flat collective

### Limitations
- Requires coordination overhead
- Working groups can become silos
- Need clear boundaries between groups

---

## Model 3: The Council Model

### How It Works
A small elected or rotating council (3-7 people) handles day-to-day decisions. Major decisions go to the full community via vote.

### Best For
- Larger groups (20-50+ people)
- Nodes with significant resources
- Communities needing faster decision-making

### Strengths
- Efficient decision-making
- Clear accountability
- Scalable

### Limitations
- Risk of power concentration
- Council may not represent community
- Requires election/rotation processes

---

## Model 4: The Cooperative

### How It Works
Formal cooperative structure with bylaws, membership requirements, and democratic governance (one member, one vote).

### Best For
- Nodes managing significant financial resources
- Long-term projects
- Groups that need legal structure

### Strengths
- Legal protection
- Established governance framework
- Can open bank accounts, sign contracts
- Member ownership

### Limitations
- Administrative overhead
- Regulatory requirements
- Setup costs and time

---

## Choosing Your Model

| Factor | Collective | Working Groups | Council | Cooperative |
|--------|-----------|---------------|---------|------------|
| Group size | 5-10 | 10-30 | 20-50+ | 15+ |
| Formality | Low | Medium | Medium-High | High |
| Decision speed | Slow | Medium | Fast | Medium |
| Setup effort | None | Low | Medium | High |
| Legal protection | None | None | None | Yes |

---

## Evolution

Most nodes evolve through models:
1. Start as a collective (5-10 people meeting informally)
2. Grow into working groups (10-20 people with different functions)
3. Add a council layer (20+ people needing faster decisions)
4. Formalize as a cooperative (when managing significant resources)

Don't skip stages. Each stage builds the relationships and governance skills needed for the next.

---

## Moving Forward

Start with the simplest model that works for your current size and purpose. Plan to evolve as you grow.
