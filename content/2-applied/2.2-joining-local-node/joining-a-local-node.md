# Joining a Local Node

*How to find and join an existing local node*

---

## Finding a Local Node

### Where to Look
- **Greenpill Network directory**: The largest network of regen-focused local nodes
- **Local cryptocurrency meetup groups**: Meetup.com, Eventbrite, Luma
- **Community currency networks**: Timebank directories, LETS networks
- **Social media**: Search "[your city] + regen" or "web3 community" on Twitter/X, Discord servers
- **University groups**: Many nodes start at universities with blockchain clubs

### What to Search For
- "[your city] timebank"
- "[your city] local currency"
- "[your city] community finance"
- "regen DAO [your region]"
- "[your city] Greenpill"

### If Nothing Exists
If there's no node near you, consider starting one. See the "Starting a Local Node" section. Or participate remotely in an existing node — many accept remote members for governance and online activities.

## Evaluating a Node

Before committing, assess fit:

### Values Alignment
- Does the node's mission resonate with yours?
- Are its practices consistent with its stated values?
- Do members treat each other with respect?

### Activity Level
- How often does the node meet or interact?
- Are there recent activities and communications?
- Is there a core group that's actively engaged?

### Governance
- How are decisions made?
- Is there transparency around finances?
- Can new members meaningfully participate?

### Culture
- Is the community welcoming to newcomers?
- Is there diversity of perspective and background?
- Do people seem genuinely connected, or is it performative?

**Red flags**: Only one person does everything. No financial transparency. Jargon-heavy communication with no effort to include newcomers. Inactive channels with months-old messages.

## The Joining Process

### Step 1: Research
Read what you can before attending. Check their website, social media, and any public documentation. Form questions.

### Step 2: Attend an Event
Show up to an open event — a meetup, a workshop, a community meal. Observe how people interact. Note who welcomes you.

### Step 3: Introduce Yourself
Share who you are, what drew you here, what skills you bring, and what you hope to get from participation. Be genuine — communities can tell when someone's performing.

### Step 4: Start Small
Don't try to change everything in your first week. Attend a few events. Join a communication channel. Help with something small. Get to know people.

### Step 5: Deepen Engagement
After a month or two, if it feels right:
- Join a working group
- Participate in governance discussions
- Propose an initiative
- Take on a small leadership role

---

## Your Rights and Responsibilities

### Rights
- To be treated with respect and dignity
- To access information about finances and governance
- To participate in decisions that affect you
- To contribute your skills and have them valued
- To leave if it's not a fit, without guilt or pressure

### Responsibilities
- Show up when you commit to showing up
- Communicate honestly and constructively
- Respect community agreements and governance processes
- Contribute what you can — time, skills, resources
- Support new members the way you were (or wish you were) supported

---

## Moving Forward

Find a node. Attend. Participate. See if it's right for you. The best communities aren't perfect — they're honest, welcoming, and committed to growing together.
