# Local Currency Design for Community Finance

*A practical guide to designing and launching a community currency*

---

## Why Local Currency?

Local currencies serve purposes that national currencies can't:

- **Keep wealth local**: When you spend locally, money circulates within the community rather than draining out
- **Value local labor**: Someone's time has value regardless of what the market rates are
- **Build community identity**: A currency creates shared symbols and rituals
- **Enable exchange when mainstream money is scarce**: During economic downturns, local currencies can maintain exchange

The most famous example is Ithaca Hours in New York, launched in 1991. One Ithaca Hour equals one hour of labor. It works because it values everyone's time equally — a hour of childcare equals an hour of accounting.

---

## Design Questions

### 1. What Is the Unit?

**Time-based**: One unit = one hour of labor. Simple, fair, intuitive. Works well for timebanks.

**Value-based**: One unit = a specific amount of value (e.g., $1 USD equivalent). Allows for skill differentials.

**Points-based**: Arbitrary units that members agree on. Most flexible but least intuitive.

**Recommendation**: Start with time-based. It's easy to understand and easy to explain. As the system matures, you can add skill multipliers.

### 2. What Backs It?

**Nothing (fiat)**: The currency has value because people trust it will be accepted. Works if the community is tight.

**Backed by labor**: Each unit represents an hour of work promised. Similar to timebanks.

**Backed by goods**: Currency can be exchanged for items in a community store.

**Backed by fiat**: Pegged to USD or another national currency. Reduces volatility but requires reserves.

**Recommendation**: Start with simple labor-backing. No reserves needed. Trust-based.

### 3. Who Issues It?

**Member-issued**: Members create currency by contributing (working, providing goods). The community collectively decides what counts.

**System-issued**: A predetermined schedule creates currency (like Bitcoin's issuance). No collective decision needed.

**Hybrid**: Some created by contribution, some by governance decision (e.g., community projects funded by newly issued currency).

**Recommendation**: Member-issued by contribution. It's the most democratic and creates natural scarcity.

### 4. What Are the Rules?

- **Who can earn?** Just members or anyone in the community?
- **What can be earned?** All labor? Only certain types?
- **What can be spent?** Anything or are there restrictions?
- **Is there expiry?** Do units lose value over time (inflation) or can they be saved?
- **Are there fees?** Transaction fees, membership fees?

**Recommendation**: Open to all. All legitimate labor counts. No spending restrictions. Some expiry or community fund allocation to prevent hoarding.

## Technical Implementation

### Option 1: No Tech (Simplest)

Paper vouchers or a simple spreadsheet ledger. Works for small communities (under 50 people).

**Pros**: No technical barriers, easy to understand, no fees

**Cons**: Manual record-keeping, harder to scale, security risks

### Option 2: Digital Ledger

A shared spreadsheet or database that tracks balances. One person or team manages it.

**Pros**: Easier tracking, can handle more members, searchable

**Cons**: Centralized point of control, requires trust in managers

### Option 3: Blockchain-Based

Smart contract or token tracks balances on-chain.

**Pros**: Transparent, trustless, programmable, can integrate with DeFi

**Cons**: Technical barriers, transaction fees, complexity

**Recommendation**: Start simple. Paper or spreadsheet for first year. Upgrade when you hit scaling challenges.

## Launch Process

### Phase 1: Design (1-2 Months)
- Discuss and decide on unit, backing, rules
- Write simple "constitution" or agreement
- Get initial commitments from 10-20 founding members

### Phase 2: Pilot (3-6 Months)
- Launch with small group
- Test earning and spending
- Iterate on rules based on experience
- Document what works

### Phase 3: Scale (Ongoing)
- Open to more members
- Formalize governance
- Consider technical upgrades

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Starting Too Complex
Don't launch with tokens, smart contracts, and DeFi integrations. Start with paper or spreadsheets.

### Mistake 2: No Spending Opportunities
Currency only has value if it can be spent. Ensure there are things to buy BEFORE you start issuing.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Legal
Some jurisdictions regulate currency issuance. Check local regulations. Most small timebanks fly under the radar, but don't assume.

### Mistake 4: No Expiry or Community Fund
Without some mechanism to put currency back in circulation, early participants hoard and new participants can't earn.

### Mistake 5: Launching Without Critical Mass
You need at least 20-30 active members for a functional currency. Start with your core community.

## Examples to Study

- **Brixton Pound** (UK): Local currency with some blockchain elements
- **Sardex** (Italy): Commercial currency for small businesses

## Moving Forward

Start simple. A currency can evolve, but it can't evolve if it never launches. Get something working with your core group, learn from experience, and iterate.
