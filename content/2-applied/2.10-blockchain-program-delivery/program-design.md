# Designing Blockchain Education Programs

*How to design effective blockchain education programs for your community.*

---

## Overview

Good program design starts with your learners, not with the technology. Too many blockchain education programs are built around what the facilitator finds interesting rather than what the community needs.

## Start with Your Audience

### Who Are Your Learners?
- **Complete beginners**: Never heard of blockchain. Need analogies and patience.
- **Curious explorers**: Heard about it, want to understand. Need clear explanations.
- **Practical adopters**: Want to use it for something specific. Need hands-on guidance.
- **Builders**: Want to create projects. Need technical depth.

### What Do They Need?
Don't ask "what should we teach?" Ask "what problems are our learners trying to solve?"

A community that wants to run a timebank needs different content than a community that wants to understand DeFi.

## Designing Your Curriculum

### The 3-Layer Approach

**Layer 1: Why (1-2 sessions)**
Why does this matter? What problems does it solve? What's the vision?
No technology yet. Just purpose and context.

**Layer 2: How (3-5 sessions)**
How does it work? Hands-on practice. Setting up wallets, making transactions, participating in governance.

**Layer 3: What Next (2-3 sessions)**
What can you build? How do you contribute? What are the next steps?

### Key Principles

1. **Start with real-world analogies**: A wallet is like a lockbox. A blockchain is like a shared notebook. Gas fees are like postage.
2. **Hands-on from session 2**: People learn by doing.
3. **Small wins early**: First wallet setup, first transaction — celebrate these.
4. **Spiral, don't lecture**: Revisit concepts multiple times with increasing depth.
5. **Leave room for questions**: Budget 30% of time for Q&A and troubleshooting.

## Practical Considerations

### Materials
- Printed quick-reference cards (wallet setup, key terms)
- Pre-configured test environments
- Video recordings for those who miss sessions
- Written guides for later reference

### Assessment
Don't test people. Instead, track:
- Can they set up a wallet independently?
- Can they explain the concept to someone else?
- Are they using the tools in practice?

### Accessibility
- Plain language, no jargon
- Multiple learning modes (visual, hands-on, reading)
- Accommodate different paces
- Provide materials in advance

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too much theory**: People tune out after 20 minutes of slides
2. **Too fast**: What takes you 5 minutes took you months to learn
3. **Assuming knowledge**: Never assume people know what an "address" is
4. **No follow-up**: Learning without practice fades fast
5. **Tech-first**: Starting with technology instead of purpose

## Moving Forward

Design for your actual learners. Start simple. Iterate based on feedback. The best program is one people actually complete and use.
